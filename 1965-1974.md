## CHAPTER I

## GOVERNMENT AND ELECTIONS

1965

### GOVERNMENT

#### CITY COUNCIL

In a letter to the editor of the "Daily Camera" dated May 18, Councilman Jack V. McDermott said:

... the current policy of the City Council is to discourage reckless growth outside the city limits. Regardless of any annexations, the area surrounding Boulder will continue to grow. The question then remains, "Do we want fragmented growth or coordinated growth?" Without annexations, the municipal resident must continue to subsidize certain municipal services such as library facilities, streets and parks for the use of outlying area citizens while the city's tax base is prevented from expanding. An examination of population statistics would seem to bear out that a good deal of planning is necessary if we are to have orderly growth. That Boulder and the immediate surrounding area's population will increase by a minimum of 12,000 to 15,000 due to IBM's announcement alone is based on experience, not imagination. This growth will continue to accelerate and only through long-range planning will we be able to maintain Boulder as a desirable community in which to live.

On June 8, the City Council adopted The Service Area Concept: A Program for Boulder's Planned Development.

The Service Area Concept involves (1) the geographic area surrounding the City of Boulder in which the City Council will consider providing water and/or sewer service to outside customers, and (2) the terms and conditions the outside customers must agree to, in order to receive either or both of these services. By offering to provide water and/or sewer services to outside customers at double inside-city rates, but only upon their willingness to agree to several stringent conditions, the City Council seeks to achieve two major objectives: (1) planned, orderly development of our city and its fringe areas, and (2) a sound, equitable financial structure for our municipal utilities.

From a "Daily Camera" article dated June 9 came these statements:

(This policy was) earlier known as the Spokes of the Wheel concept. ... (The Service Area Concept) was prepared by Councilman Howard Klemme, who incorporated into it the thinking of his fellow council members. ... As Councilman Robert Knecht said, "The concept is pioneering. No other city we know of has tried to use their basic

utility services as such an important tool for long-range planning."

In an article dated June 28, about the July 20 city election, Dick Walberg of the "Daily Camera" reported:

For Boulder, with its two biggest industries, the University and the National Bureau of Standards, tax-free, the desirability of industrial lands within the city limits is apparent. City water and sewer service for industrial land outside Boulder, tying it unalterably to annexation as soon as legally possible, is part and parcel of the service area concept.

A University of Colorado study of Boulder's economy showed that science-related industry and the University have been the two major factors in the city's recent phenomenal growth. The growth of Boulder included a doubling of population and employment figures, a tripling of retail sales and a 400 per cent increase in total assessed valuation in the city in the last 14 years.

In September, the Colorado Supreme Court upheld the City of Boulder's annexation of the Crossroads Shopping Center. The case had been in the courts for nearly four years. The City Council annexed the 37-acre site in September 1961. In November of that year, two downtown property owners filed suit on behalf of themselves and others to void the annexation.

#### CITY MANAGER

The "Denver Post" of July 28 reported the following partial account of an interview with E. Robert Turner, outgoing City Manager of Boulder:

In five and a half years there has been a great change in the attitude of Boulder people about growth; most of them accept it, but in my opinion they don't go far enough. ... Now that they accept growth as here, and continuing, they have to realize the growth must be controlled through utilities. ... All this trying to plan ahead is difficult because people don't want to think of Boulder as a city of 250,000 in 40 years  
... .

#### CITY ATTORNEY

In a "Daily Camera" article dated August 10, it was reported that a damage suit against the city by owners of property near the former city dump was settled out of court. City Attorney Peter C. Dietze said the case was compromised at \$5,000, of which the city would pay \$4,000 and the United Haulers Association \$1,000.

#### MUNICIPAL COURT

An article dated December 15 related that a panel of six high-school students, selected as Boulder's first Youth Jury, was in Municipal Court to sit in judgement of their contemporaries, teen-age drivers charged with traffic offenses. The jurors acted only as an advisory body to Judge Richard G. Hansen. Sponsored and organized by the Junior Chamber of Commerce, they represented Boulder and Fairview High Schools and Mount St. Gertrude's Academy.

#### ELECTIONS

Three elections were held in the city in 1965. The first was on March 30 to decide two issues. Would the voters approve a \$3 million bond issue for sewer system improvements? Would they approve a franchise for city-wide trash and garbage collection? The results were: For the sewer bonds, 3,738; against, 3,848. For the trash franchise, 2,289; against, 5,297.

The "Camera" dated March 31 carried an article which said:

The Monday morning quarterbacks are already at it. The sewer bonds went down, they speculated, because: The people don't want Boulder to grow; the vote was a strike against the "Spokes of the Wheel" concept; the people are up to their ears in taxes and the costs of public service, and this is the time of the year income and property taxes are paid; the people, in voting overwhelmingly against the refuse

franchise, swept the sewer bonds down with it.

The second election was on July 20. It repeated the request for \$3 million for the sewer improvement program. On June 14, the State Board of Health had allowed the city 90 days to correct violations of sewer plant effluent standards. An added ballot measure was a citizen-initiated item challenging an ordinance passed by the Council which had approved an extension of water service to 1600 acres owned by Mr. Sam Rudd beyond the southeastern border of Boulder. The results: Those for the sewer bonds, 3,880; those against, 2,635. Those for the water extension, 3,298; those against, 3,644.

A "Camera" editorial on July 21 stated:

Boulder citizens deserve praise for passing the sewer bond proposal with a strong margin of 1,245 votes. It was the same plan turned down last March by a thin 110 vote margin. The second issue, the service extension contract, was defeated by a small margin. We do not think the defeat was a result of any fault in the service area concept. It is evident that there is still much confusion over this new approach to the control of urban growth.

Another article on the election in the same issue of the "Camera" stated:

Councilman Robert Knecht, who cast the single dissenting vote on Council approval of an ordinance authorizing the water extension contract, viewed the election results as approving, in general, the (Service Area) concept, but not the specific contract proposed. "The developer (Sam L. Rudd of Denver) did not present firm development plans, and this is why I and the voters questioned city water service to his land," Knecht said. He pointed out plans for the other two extensions, one to Gunbarrel Greens and the International Business Machines Corporation plant to the northeast, and the other out East Arapahoe, came to the council accompanied by firm development plans.

The third election, on November 2, was for five Council members from a slate of 17 candidates.

1966

### GOVERNMENT

#### CITY COUNCIL

In a "Camera" article dated February 2, it was reported:

Recognizing Boulder's growth and future expansion, the City Council "... gave the green light to applying for a federal grant to study the city's present posture and to sketch a comprehensive growth plan." Under a \$125,000 program, which would be financed two-thirds by federal and one-third by city funds, 18 to 24 months would be spent on the study.

#### CITY MANAGER

The "Camera" of November 22 announced the resignation of City Manager Robert W. Quinlan. He had been manager since September 15, 1965. Mayor Robert W. Knecht told the "Camera" that Quinlan's decision came as a result of a series of "serious discussions" with the Council and that during the discussions it had become Mr. Quinlan's opinion that the role he was able to play in directing the city administration had not worked out to his satisfaction, and the Council concurred.

#### CITY ATTORNEY

A memorandum dated September 14 from the City Attorney to the City Council listed 26 items of pending litigation involving the city. Five of these were concerned with claims against a contractor for repairs to the Silver Lake Dam. Ten more were condemnation actions to obtain rights-of-way for sewer mains and for the widening of Baseline Road. Another action involved the validity of an ordinance that required refuse haulers to dump trash collected from within the city at the County disposal site south of Marshall. Others were actions: (1) challenging the granting of an exception by the Board of Zoning Adjustment for the purpose of constructing a drive-in restaurant at the southwest corner of Table Mesa Drive and Broadway;

(2) challenging the city's title to a tract of land just east of Chautauqua Reservoir; (3) challenging the decision of the Malt Beverage Licensing Authority to approve a license for a new 3.2 beer outlet to be constructed at the southeast corner of Basemar Shopping Center; and (4) challenging the city's authority to rent certain property on the northwest corner of Broadway and Spruce Street for the purpose of providing off-street parking.

#### ELECTIONS

No city-wide elections were held in 1966.

1967

### GOVERNMENT

#### CITY COUNCIL

The "Daily Camera" of January 4 stated:

Boulder will move this year to annex all or parts of 11 of the 14 county islands within the city limits. 1967 is the first year in which cities, under a state law, can initiate unilateral annexation of county islands and peninsulas, if two-thirds abutting the city, that have existed for three years. ... Areas proposed for annexation under the statute exceed 110 acres in all and have an assessed valuation in excess of a quarter of a million dollars, according to City Planning Director James Bowers. (City) policy calls for annexation whenever it is beneficial to the city and does not put someone out of business, such as liquor establishment owners.

On May 10, the new home of the National Center for Atmospheric Research was dedicated. During the ceremonies it was stated that Boulder was chosen for the center because Dr. Walter Orr Roberts, who was selected as its director, wanted to remain in Boulder. More than 300 staff and board members attended the event.

On September 27, the "Camera" reported on a study session of the Council:

Giving impetus for Boulder's growth to the north through the installation of a \$400,000 sewer line and a \$70,000 water line, and a possible property tax rate reduction were the main topics of consideration by City Council. City Manager Ted Tedesco pointed out that the hoped-for development in north Boulder was not taking place, and the city, instead of waiting for developers to start the growth, must provide the services to stimulate that area. The question of lowering the mill levy had come up in a separate meeting on the proposed sales tax increase of one per cent to provide funds for the purchase of "open space" and for the development of a major thoroughfare plan. Mayor Robert Knecht ... proposed that the Council look into a possible two mill reduction in the present levy. This would be an advantage to taxpayers in addition to a proposed food sales tax rebate of \$4.67 a year. The proposal would also shift some of the burden to those who reside outside the city and make purchases here, since they would not be able to collect the rebate or benefit from the property tax cut.

In June the Beverage Licensing Authority was updating city laws to conform with the state statutes. These prohibited alcoholic beverage outlets within 500 feet of an educational institution. Exceptions in Boulder were the "Sink" and the "Tulagi" which had had licenses before the state law was in effect. In a "Denver Post" article of December 13, Barbara Gigone wrote:

Six county liquor "islands" within the city of Boulder have been annexed by the City Council. Each piece of property contains a liquor outlet and could not come into the city until a referendum dissolving the ban on liquor sales was passed on November 7.

The "islands" were listed in the article as: Harvest House Hotel, Lamp Post Restaurant, Joratz property, Pudlik Liquor Store, Baseline Liquor Store, and the Mountain-Vu Liquor Store. The first application for an in-city liquor license was filed minutes after the November election results were certified. The applicant was Tom Lacey for the Liquor Mart.

#### CITY MANAGER

On June 1, the "Daily Camera" headlined "Open Door Policy at City Hall":

"This door will stay open", Boulder's new city manager declared. The new manager, Francis J. (Ted) Tedesco, pointed to the oak-paneled door ... between his inner office and the outer office occupied by his secretary, Mrs. Catherine Springsteel. The door, he continued, will be open to the public, the council, and the staff. Only on rare occasions, when he is involved in a personnel matter, will the door be closed.

In a "Camera" article on September 8, a letter from City Manager Tedesco to the Boulder Valley School District was quoted:

Concern has been raised recently by school officials regarding the city administration's proposal to implement greenbelt and major thoroughfare programs for the city of Boulder. The effect of this proposal, it is felt, will harm the program for school construction. ... The use of the sales tax as the mechanism for achieving these programs was recommended primarily because it would not conflict with the property tax levy which is the primary source of revenue to the School District. It is recognized that the School District will call upon the property tax for its needed funds and therefore the city can best look to other sources of revenue so as not to place an increased burden on property taxpayers. In addition, the sales tax best reflects those individuals who gain a benefit from the city's facilities i.e. students, visitors, county residents, etc. The sales tax can also be designed so that it is not regressive in its application.

#### CITY ATTORNEY

A "Camera" article dated March 24 stated that "Boulder is party to 22 lawsuits". According to a report by City Attorney Peter Dietze, nine of these were routine condemnation actions to determine the price of rights-of-way, four were about the troubled Silver Lake Dam project, and the remainder involved a variety of subjects.

The project to raise the height of the dam at Silver Lake, in the city watershed above Nederland, had been stalled midway to completion in November 1965, when the contractor went bankrupt. Various subcontractors had filed claims for labor, materials and supplies and had named the city

among the defendants.

Several local citizens had challenged the city's authority to enter into a 20-year lease agreement for the rental of property at Broadway and Spruce Street for a parking lot. District Judge William E. Buck had dismissed the complaint in March 1966, but his decision had been appealed to the state's Supreme Court. A lawsuit against a proposal to pave streets in a 42 block area had also been dismissed in District Court, but an appeal was expected. The plaintiff had been unsuccessful in a similar court action and appeal in 1965.

In April, the City Attorney filed a complaint on behalf of Boulder to bring about compliance with the city zoning laws, which were allegedly violated by the owners and developers of the K-Mart Discount Store.

A memorandum dated June 14 from the City Attorney to the City Council was prepared "to attempt to answer some of the questions regarding what liability, if any, the city assumes by annexing a parcel of property on which a liquor license is to be exercised". This was one of the problems of annexing County islands. In this case, a pending application for a state liquor license for a proposed restaurant located on the Joratz property stood in the way of annexation. The local licensing authority (Board of County Commissioners), had already recommended in favor of that license.

In December of 1967, the City Attorney's office prepared an ordinance titled "An ordinance controlling the excesses of certain elements within the city limits of the City of Boulder; prohibiting unreasonable wind velocities; providing exceptions for designated amounts of wind on designated days; and declaring an emergency therefor".

#### ELECTIONS

Two elections were held in Boulder in 1967. The first was on April 4. Three million dollars in bonds were approved, this time to provide a second water treatment plant, a distribution reservoir in south Boulder and facilities accessory to these projects. The outcome was 3,388 in favor; 1,671 against.

In the "Daily Camera" of April 5, Acting Mayor Howard Klemme was quoted as saying at the Council meeting, "Today's election dealt with one stage of development in one of the city's services, but it was a key vote, not only because water is so vital, but because the philosophy of long-range planning has clearly been recognized as the key to our future development." In the same issue, Mayor Knecht said that during the month-long campaign he found the public concerned with Boulder's growth and its optimum size.

On November 7, citizens went to the polls to vote for five Council persons from a slate of eight. Two ballot issues pulled out a large number of voters. The local option liquor sale passed with a 71 per cent majority of 9,701 for and 3,965 against. Since 1909, Boulder had voted fifteen times to prohibit the sale of alcoholic beverages in the city. According to a November 13th "Camera" article by R. Roger Harkins, the "dry" majority had become progressively smaller in each of the past four elections, and in this year the "wets" won by a margin of 5,736. Various theories were cited for the change: A wave of permissiveness; a mandate for Mayor Robert W. Knecht; IBM influence.

In 1975, Timothy Lange of the "Colorado Daily" staff did a series of articles on Boulder election history. In the October 28th edition of that year, he wrote about the November 1967 liquor vote:

Because of continuous expansion of the city limits, there were within the city boundaries "liquor islands" (and) just outside the city limits were nine retail liquor outlets. The question (on the ballot), according to supporters of the "wet" proposal, was two-fold: to end the hypocrisy of saying Boulder was "dry", and to gain \$40,000 in liquor sales revenue. The supporters also said that liquor licenses would help revitalize some downtown restaurants. This issue, said supporters such as the Citizen's Committee for Tax Equity, was purely economic, not moral. A few citizens (who) opposed the sale of liquor within the city limits ... were ... owners of "island" and outside "city limits" liquor stores, and they had launched a strong ... anti-wet campaign.

The second ballot issue was the Greenbelt/Major Thoroughfare program which added one cent to the sales tax. This issue passed with 8,375 voting "yes" and 5,268 voting "no", a 61 per cent majority in favor.

From the same "Colorado Daily" article cited came the following comments:

The greenbelt plan, said backers, would provide a 12,500 acre "breathing space" around the Boulder valley. The first priority would be the mountain backdrop, about 5,000 acres, where developers were already beginning to plan for subdivisions. ... The city sales tax would, indeed, be increased but each resident would receive a yearly food-tax rebate and the property tax would be cut two mills. ... Most of the sales tax opposition came from people who did not believe that their property tax would go down at the same time the sales tax went up. ... But the greenbelt supporters were riding the crest of an increasing wave of environmental concern.

1968

### GOVERNMENT

#### CITY COUNCIL

A "Denver Post" article dated February 14 indicated that the Council was involved with annexations of more parcels of land on the fringes of the

city and with implementation of the Major Thoroughfare program.

Boulder's city limits never changed from 1908 to 1946. In 1946 there were 2.7 square miles within the city. By 1950, there were 3.1 and in 1960, 7.2. Going into 1968, the city was well on the way to doubling its size again with annexations during 1967 placing Boulder's size at 12.6 square miles.

The Director of Public Works, Andy Briscoe, listed for the Council the Major Thoroughfare program projects to be funded through earmarked sales tax revenue: A study of 28th and Baseline intersection; construction at Ninth and University; a project to widen and extend 24th Street north; a connecting route from Pearl and Spruce to the new 47th Street freeway; reconstruction of Iris Avenue; the widening of Baseline Road from 30th Street to the freeway; and the city's share in Colorado Highway Department projects.

In January the Liquor Licensing Authority was setting applicant priorities for liquor licenses. State law set the basis on which a license decision must be made as "meeting the reasonable needs of the neighborhood". City Attorney Dietze suggested that the "neighborhood" be the area from which an outlet can expect to draw its patrons. In the final analysis, the Authority defined the neighborhood for each application separately.

In April, the Authority gave tentative approval to Tom's Tavern at 1047 Pearl Street. Keller's at 1027 Pearl had also received preliminary sanction. The third application was at 1102 Pearl Street, Walt and Hank's Tavern. On June 4, after three months of deliberation, a liquor license was granted to Keller's, while licenses for Tom's Tavern and Walt and Hank's were denied on the basis that the needs of the neighborhood were served. The owners of Tom's Tavern and Walt and Hank's Tavern filed

lawsuits against the License Authority asking for judicial review. In September and October, the decisions of the Authority were upheld by District Judge Howard O. Ashton, and later in the year, motions for new trials were denied.

The first city-issued liquor license in more than 60 years was signed on May 1 for a new establishment, the Liquor Mart, at 1712 Pearl Street.

#### CITY MANAGER

In January, City Manager Tedesco hired a new assistant city manager. Larry N. Blick was put in charge of budget and research.

According to a "Denver Post" article dated March 6: Ted Tedesco said the city has little choice but to hire a consultant for advice and recommendations on the Public Service Company utility franchise. The franchise comes up for renewal in a general election every 20 years. The Company was first contracted for utility service in 1929. Tedesco said he would continue to lead the city's negotiation team, but he would be armed with information from David Koch, a Washington, D.C. utilities consultant. The City Council agreed to pay the Koch firm up to \$30,000 for the work.

In May, Mr. Tedesco reorganized the Public Works and Utilities Departments. The Public Works Department was divided into four main divisions: transportation, design, operations, and building and zoning under Public Works Director Andy Briscoe. The Utilities Department was separated into six divisions: water, sewer, watershed operations, planning and development, meters, and maintenance, under Utilities Director William Light.

In August, the City Manager announced plans to partially consolidate the Boulder Police and Fire Departments under a Public Safety Officer program. The plan, the result of three months of research and discussion,

called for the retention of separate Police and Fire Departments. However, 40 patrolmen would be trained as firefighters and four firemen would be trained as policemen.

The firefighters began circulating petitions in October seeking a City Charter amendment to prohibit consolidation of the departments without a vote of the people. They also asked for a vote on the questions of a reduction in their work week, and a twelve-and-a-half per cent across-the-board pay increase effective in April 1969. In November, the City Manager said his plan would go into effect in January on a trial basis. He said the issues of hours and pay were subjects for negotiation, not for election.

In December, a memo to the City Manager set out the Goals for the Boulder Community which were developed by members of the various boards and commissions in conjunction with the administrative staff. The formulation of goals had begun in March with a gathering of more than 100 interested citizens at a "Goals Seminar" held at the National Center for Atmospheric Research.

#### CITY ATTORNEY

In March, the City Attorney presented a memorandum to the City Council with suggestions for criteria concerning requests for expansion, enlargement or modification of existing liquor outlets. In May, he submitted recommended procedures for acquiring real property for rights-of-way, open space, parks and other purposes.

In August, City Attorney Walter Wagenhals was scheduled to appear before a three-judge federal court to argue on behalf of Boulder's Mayor and police officers. The court would rule on the constitutionality of Colorado's vagrancy law. The case stemmed from a raid on a "hippie flop-

house" in Boulder.

In a "Camera" article dated September 25, it was announced that City Attorney Wagenhals would argue two cases before the Colorado Supreme Court; one involving an annexation dispute over a trout farm near 26th Street and Valmont Road (Cline property); the other an appeal from residents in north Boulder who had tried to incorporate a new municipality of "North Boulder" in 1966, and whose petition to incorporate was thrown out of court. Mr. Wagenhals said the city had fought the incorporation to avoid a metropolitan area with many jurisdictions, such as exists in Denver and its surrounding counties and cities.

#### ELECTIONS

There were no in-city elections in 1968.

1969

### GOVERNMENT

#### CITY COUNCIL

In January, the City Council reluctantly set February 11 for an election on a Charter amendment to prohibit consolidation of the Police and Fire Departments. Mayor Robert Knecht appointed Councilmen Newkirk, Trent, and Bowers to a subcommittee of the Council to plan a campaign against the amendment. Two weeks later, the Council repealed the ordinance setting the special election. An agreement had been reached between the city and the firefighters, and the five firemen who circulated the petitions calling for the election asked that the petitions be withdrawn.

In July, the Charter Committee of the Council recommended that two amendments be placed on the ballot in November; one changing the language

on the powers of the Board of Zoning Adjustment and the other dealing with the three per cent limitation on bonded indebtedness.

In May, the Council passed a resolution formally verifying citizen's petitions asking for a study of the costs of acquiring and operating the gas and electric utilities in Boulder. In July, City Manager Tedesco received authority from the Council to select a consultant for that four to five month feasibility study. A month later the City Council authorized \$30,000 to be spent for the study of city acquisition of the utility. The agreement with R. W. Beck stated that Beck would obtain Public Service Company records pertaining to the operations of the electric system and make a summary inventory of the property and facilities subject to acquisition. The study, to be completed by December 1, would be submitted to the city's Special Utility Study Committee, which would review it and make recommendations to the Council.

State statutes required unsuccessful applicants for liquor licenses to wait two years before reapplying. Tom's Tavern and Walt and Hank's had applied for liquor licenses in June 1968, but were denied on grounds that the one existing license met the needs of the neighborhood. They would be eligible to reapply in June 1970.

In July, the Council issued a proclamation setting aside Monday, the 21st, as a Day of Participation for the people of the City of Boulder to honor the Apollo 11 moon landing.

In November, the Council appointed three of its members, Richard Geesaman, James Bowers, and John Buechner to a city-school coordinating committee. The committee was to meet monthly to discuss mutual problems of the city and the Boulder Valley School District.

#### CITY MANAGER

In March, City Manager Tedesco proposed that the requirements for receiving city sewer and water services be amended so that property owners outside the city who hook onto city utilities conform to the concepts of the Boulder Valley Comprehensive Plan. "This would allow us to use the extension of the utilities to control growth, not accommodate it", Tedesco said. The policy would back off from an aggressive annexation program and would allow city officials to study proposed annexations in light of their total benefit to the city.

In July, the City Manager reorganized the administration to place all functions of city government under three major departments: Administrative Services, Community Development, and Public Facilities. Heading these departments would be Larry Blick, Bill Lamont, and Andy Briscoe.

Eighteen recommendations to improve communication between City Hall and Boulder citizens had been made to the City Manager based on a survey of citizen attitudes toward city government concluded in the spring by Dacey, Wolf, and Weir, a Boulder advertising firm.

#### MUNICIPAL COURT

A "Camera" article of July 16 by Pete Goter stated: "A new Boulder law which prohibits sleeping in cars, passed as an emergency ordinance about two weeks ago, may not last another two weeks. #### ELECTIONS

One election was held in Boulder in 1969 on November 4. From 17 City Council candidates, five were chosen. Also on the ballot were questions of whether to fluoridate Boulder's water, and two Charter changes. The fluoridation question won by a vote of 3,257 to 2,979. The first Charter amendment, giving the City Council the power to determine by ordinance the authority of the Board of Zoning Adjustment, won by a vote of 3,296 to 2,708. The second amendment, which passed by a vote of 3,439 to 2,549, allowed Boulder to exclude from the city's three per cent debt limit bonds issued and retired with revenues from the city's sales tax, special taxes, and other revenues, except those from the property tax. Under the previous Charter section, bonds totalling more than three per cent of the city's assessed valuation could not be floated regardless of what revenues were used to pay off the bonds.

"Citizens for Greenbelts" campaigned actively for the passage of Charter amendment number two. The leaders of this organization were: A. A.

Bartlett, Kurt Gerstle, Robert McKelvey, Campbell Robertson, Jo Robertson, Oakleigh Thorne, and Ruth Wright.

In the "Colorado Daily" of October 29, 1975, Timothy Lange wrote:

The campaign and election of 1969 marked the beginning of the uptight years in Boulder. Though there were controversial issues prior to that year's election, there had not previously been the bitterness and vehemence that came in 1969. ... This vehemence was aroused by the fluoridation issue which was on the ballot and by other non-ballot issues which were discussed by the widely divergent candidates for city council. Hippies, housing, drugs, discrimination, and the Boulder Tomorrow plan for the downtown area were hotly debated. On election day the toughest "conservatives" lost, but the "liberals" came in last, except for lawyer Richard McLean.

In April, the Council had approved a resolution recognizing the "outstanding efforts of the community of Boulder in the drive for Food First for Holly Springs". #### MUNICIPAL COURT

A "Camera" article dated January 3 by Larry Knudsen reported that a recent Colorado Supreme Court decision had forced the Boulder Municipal Court to obey a City Charter provision that hadn't been obeyed since 1958. The high court had ruled that people charged with petty offenses were not entitled to a trial by jury. The court defined "petty offenses" as those punishable by less than six months in jail and a \$300 fine. In Municipal

Court, this meant that there would be no more jury trials until action was taken to amend the Charter which stated emphatically that "there shall be no trial by jury ...". However, during the past 12 years there had been jury trials in Boulder Municipal Court because of ordinances that were added to the city code following another Supreme Court decision in 1958 which stated that anyone accused of violating city ordinances was entitled to all state and federal rights. Prior to 1958, there had been no jury trials in the Municipal Court.

In July, charges against 14 of the 16 persons arrested at the youth hostel at Sixth and Canyon had been dismissed in Municipal Court. The dismissal was granted because the defendants had been charged with the wrong offense.

#### ELECTIONS

In 1970 there were two special elections in Boulder, one on June 23 and one on August 4.

In May, the City Council had called for a June election to decide on a \$7.4 million civic center bond issue to construct the first two phases of the proposed Boulder Tomorrow civic center: A 500-seat theater, new city hall, 100 parking spaces and a plaza for \$2,750,000; and a conference, meeting and exhibition space, 200 parking spaces, and remodeling of the existing city hall for a public safety building for \$1.8 million. Included would be flood control, water and sewer facilities, replacement of park facilities and money for land acquisition for the civic center. According to a "Daily Camera" article dated May 6, Assistant City Manager Larry Blick said the bond issue would require a 2 mill property tax levy for an indeterminate length of time. (Mayor) Knecht ... noted that if space for a

Senior Citizen's Activity Center is provided ... as planned, the city might be eligible for matching federal funds for that much of the project.

The second June ballot question went one step further than the Charter amendment that had been approved in the November 1969 election. This one would have allowed the city to pledge up to 50 per cent of the annual revenues of earmarked funds to repay bonds without a vote of the people. Councilman Howard Klemme, a member of the Charter Committee which had drafted the Charter amendment put to voters last fall, said that Committee had rejected the idea behind the newly proposed amendment as politically unhealthy. "I have qualms about eliminating the vote. I think we can get the same flexibility without amending the Charter."

A third issue was a referendum question calling for immediate United States withdrawal from Southeast Asia.

The civic center issue went down 7,578 to 2,837; the Charter amendment was defeated with 6,743 "no" votes to 3,340 "for"; and the war referendum was defeated 4,690 to 4,401.

The second special election, in August, dealt with two related issues: A City of Boulder franchise to allow the Public Service Company of Colorado to provide electric, gas and bus services and to operate within the public rights-of-way for a period of twenty years; and the imposition of an excise tax of one mill/kilowatt hour of electricity sold, to be used solely for the purpose of undergrounding wires. Negotiations on the franchise had started in January 1969, and election dates had been set and cancelled twice. The outcome: 8,452 voted for and 1,149 voted against the franchise; 3,027 voted for and 6,439 voted against the excise tax.

In the "Camera" of February 25, Ron Tollefson reported Councilman Richard McLean's view on growth policy. The policy would be one in which growth is planned for, but not encouraged. (will be) ... Boulder's growth pattern."

The advent of the 18-year-old vote in 1971 came as a bit of a shock to the old fraternity that had controlled the Boulder City Council for two decades. The clash, and the exchange of ideas which went with it, made '71 the liveliest campaign in 25 years.

Previous years had seen serious talk about growth control and about preserving the environment. But '71 brought both into sharp focus for the first time. ### GOVERNMENT

#### CITY COUNCIL

In January, newly appointed Councilwoman Janet Roberts stated that the selection of a new city manager and finding a means to control growth were the most pressing problems facing the City Council.

three-year residency requirement for City Council members, written into law in 1956, is now unenforceable, according to City Attorney Wagenhals." On February 27, Koltnow reported that the Executive Committee of the Chamber of Commerce would go to court in an effort to "compel" City Council to enforce the City Charter and the existing three-year residency requirement.

The following day, District Attorney Alex Hunter, impatient with City Council inaction on the residency question, filed a District Court suit challenging Councilman Tim Fuller's right to hold office. The District Attorney said he was seeking a speedy answer to a question that had been dividing the community. The Supreme Court declined to act on Boulder's case, but voided the residency requirement in the Colorado Springs case. In April, the City Council overturned its own six-week-old decision not to enforce the residency provision of the City Charter. In May, the District Attorney announced he would move to dismiss the District Court suit challenging Councilman Fuller's right to hold office. His decision was based on the Colorado Supreme Court's ruling that Aspen's three-year residency requirement for city candidates was unconstitutional.

In May, the Council's Charter Revision Committee announced that they would suggest placing on the ballot: A proposal for compensation of Council members; a proposal to lower the age for Council candidates from 25 to 18; an optional question on the residency requirement giving the voters a choice between 32-days or one-year; and one that would lengthen the present deadline for filing nominating petitions.

In January, the Chamber of Commerce had held a meeting on election processes. Boulder realtor, Walter Slack, a member of the Board of Directors of Citizens United to Restore Boulder (CURB), said he felt the

ward system was the best so that people could feel that they had someone to talk to. Harvey Platts, a former councilman, felt the ward system was a poor one because it causes narrow interests and results in political logrolling. In June, petitions submitted by the Committee for Fair Representation were ruled valid. They called for a Charter amendment election on whether to change Boulder's elective process from the at-large method to a district system. The "frequently-divided Council" united in unanimously condemning the proposal that would split the city into three districts, according to an article in the "Camera" on June 5 by Barry Koltnow.

In January, the Boulder Area Growth Study Commission was told to accept direction from the administration. Planning Director William Lamont was not satisfied with the Commission reports required to meet grant contract requirements. In March, the United States Department of Housing and Urban Development extended the grant until October 31. Sandy Cooper, the Commission's Chairwoman, said the group had selected four "model futures" to be researched as part of the one-year study.

In January, Larry Knudson of the "Camera" reported the Colorado Court of Appeals had ruled that the petitions to stop annexation of the Wonderland Hill subdivision were valid. If that ruling stood, the City Council would have to submit the annexation in question to the voters. In May, the City Council voted six to three to repeal the Wonderland Hill annexation ordinance almost one year after they had approved it.

In August, the Council approved a measure annexing 62 acres east of the city at 33rd Street and Arapahoe Avenue for use as an automobile sales and service area.

In a "Camera" article on December 18, Barry Koltnow reported that a

professional survey of the Gunbarrel area had demonstrated a major split among residents on the question of whether the area should be annexed by the City of Boulder or kept in its present state. The survey, conducted by Laird Landon of the University of Colorado, showed 40 per cent supporting retention of the status quo (services provided on a contractual basis).

In May, the City Council voted to hire a professional survey group to make an in-depth study of residents' attitudes, priorities and goals. In August, Barry Koltnow of the "Camera" reported the results of the opinion survey. Boulder citizens felt that public protection, pollution control and bikeways were the three programs to which the city should give the highest priority.

In November, the Council passed a controversial resolution urging Congress to inquire thoroughly and expeditiously into the question of President Nixon's impeachment.

By the middle of next month, at least a dozen top agency heads or professional-level staff members will have left the employ of the city government during a period encompassing little more than a year. ... On February 6, Bill Lamont, the Director of Community Development, was named Acting City Manager. In March, Arthur D. Little Inc., the consultant firm conducting the search for a new city manager, gave City Council a list of 114 applicants with only five given a top recommendation for the job. In April, Archie J. Twitchell was named to fill the vacancy by a five to four vote of the Council.

The new City Manager was kept busy during his first six months in filling vacancies in top-level city positions.

#### CITY ATTORNEY

In January, Assistant City Attorney Gilbert Sackheim wrote a memorandum concerning Sherrelwood Estates Planned Residential Development (PRD). The project, first approved in January 1969, was delayed by the City's Flood Plain Study which was not completed until about July 1971. Because of this, the developer, Jack Fowler, was allowed to extend his completion date to July 1974.

In February, the City Attorney informed the Council that a subsection of the Boulder ordinance on foul language had been declared unconstitutional by the District Court. Also in February, he presented a memorandum to council on the subject of the residency requirement. In it

he stated:

I must conclude ... that if the matter went to court, the court would hold our 3-year residency requirement unenforceable. I do not think it is necessary to go to court to have decided what I predict the decision will be. ... If the Council does wish to take it to court, I should be so instructed.

In a memorandum to the City Attorney from Deputy City Attorney Ronald Porter, it was reported that the complaint filed with the Human Rights Office alleging discrimination by Myers and Company in the laying off of certain employees because of the length of their hair should be dismissed. Federal officials had agreed to accept the case under the National Labor Relations Act.

#### ELECTIONS

Two city elections were held in Boulder in 1973. On September 11, a proposal to divide Boulder into districts was defeated by 29 votes, 5,054 in favor to 5,083 against. Also defeated were the following proposals: To lower the residency requirement for Council members from three years to 32 days, 1,400 for to 7,931 against; and to lower the age requirement for Council members from 25 to 18 years old, 2,761 for to 7,214 against. Winning was a proposal to change the 30 days prior to an election as the first day to file a nominating petition and 15 days prior as the last day, to 45 and 22 days, respectively. This won by a 6,969 for to a 2,435 against vote. A one-year residency requirement won by a 7,582 "yes" to a 2,280 "no" vote. This replaced a three-year residency requirement for Council candidates.

On November 6, six Council members were chosen from 18 candidates. The number of Council members elected was due to the resignation of John Buechner in February. The Charter states that:

If there shall be vacancies to be filled at a general municipal election, other than those occurring due to the expiration of a regular term, the vacancy term shall be for two years and additional councilmen shall be elected until there shall be a council of nine councilmen.

There were also two issues on the November ballot. The first was to expand the earmarking of the 60 per cent of one cent of the sales tax for transportation and not just for construction of major thoroughfares. Councilman Kenneth Wright, who initiated the ballot question, said in a "Daily Camera" article of November 4, that if the proposal passed, eventually some of the funds previously designated strictly for construction would be allocated for maintenance of thoroughfares and also for bikeways and mass transportation. This question was approved with 51.7 per cent of the votes, or 7,369 to 6,862. The second issue was on compensation for members of the Council. Councilman Richard Geesaman sponsored this pay (\$300/month) question which was defeated by a negative vote of 70.4 per cent or 4,309 to 10,235.

According to a "Town and Country Review" article dated November 7, the student vote was only about 40 per cent of what it had been in 1971.

In a "Colorado Daily" article by Timothy Lange dated October 31, 1975, the suggested reason for the poor voter turnout in 1973 was: "frustration over government in Washington tumbled into Boulder. There was also frustration because the 1971 council had not gotten a whole lot accomplished what with all the divisive community battles and the period it took to learn the ropes."

1974

### GOVERNMENT

#### CITY COUNCIL

At the time when the Boulder Area Growth Study Commission was presenting its recommendations to Council, the Boulder District Court was considering the Robinson case. (Robinson contended that the city's growth policy was unconstitutional because it restricted freedom of movement and was too vague). In a summary of Council's response to the Growth Study Commission in February were proposals for action on the state and county levels, a statement that forces affecting growth were beyond the control of the city, and that rapid growth created negative effects on the quality of life especially in the areas of transportation, housing and land use.

At an April study session, a majority of Council expressed the desire to modify the city's growth control policy because not enough low- and moderate-income housing was being provided. In a District Court decision in May, Judge Neighbors ruled that the city's interim growth policy, formulated in 1972, was legal provided a permanent, less vague policy was adopted within a reasonable amount of time. However, he ordered that urban services be extended to the Robinson parcel in the Gunbarrel area, a ruling based on the fact that the city had made itself a public utility in the provision of water and sewer and could not deny those services capriciously. In a five to four vote, the City Council in October decided to continue its fight in the controversial Robinson case. City Attorney Wagenhals was to take the case to the Colorado Supreme Court.

In September, the Council informally approved the preliminary goals of

#### a Growth Management System.

The long-standing Gunbarrel utilities contract with the city stipulated the city could annex the area when it wished. However, residents wanted a time table from the city for providing services and amenities, and some wanted immediate representation on City Council. A further problem was that Boulder residents might seek a vote on annexation if costs to them appeared too high. In an October 25th memorandum to the City Council from the Director of Community Development, it was said: that the planning staff recommended that the city formally commit itself to the annexation; that the final decision as to which annexation route (to make the area one-sixth contiguous to the city) was most feasible be delayed until a formal growth policy was adopted; that a Gunbarrel Area Citizen's Advisory Committee be constituted as soon as possible; and that efforts be made to accomplish the annexation of the entire area by 1980. On October 30 the Council reaffirmed its goal to annex the area and approved the formation of an advisory committee.

On November 10, Jane Cracraft reported in the "Denver Post" that:

Gunbarrel's residents had been told 10 years ago they'd be annexed to Boulder as soon as the city limits touched it. But growth control intervened, and there's still a four mile gap (between the city and its satellite community.) To be eligible for annexation, one-sixth of the boundaries of a piece of property must touch the city limits. So first, the city must annex greenbelt land, step-by-step. ... Each procedure requires six months or so, and planners estimate it will be a three to five year process. ... Residents of the Gunbarrel area are asking now for urban-type services. ... A survey last spring showed that 44 per cent of the residents are eager to annex to Boulder, 31 per cent prefer the status quo, 5 per cent want to incorporate as an independent city, 7 per cent prefer establishing a special service district to provide fire protection and other services, while 13 per cent are "undecided".

In March, a group announced that it would seek a recall election of the five City Council members who voted in favor of the sexual orientation ordinance. By the end of the month they reported that copies of a petition to recall Mayor Penfield Tate and Councilman Tim Fuller were being printed.

In May, a District Court suit was filed to have the recall election postponed until fall. The complaint alleged that the petition did not present substantial grounds for removal from office and that if the election were held in June, many Colorado University students would be disenfranchised. A temporary restraining order blocking the City Council from setting June 11 as a recall date was issued. The City Council gave unanimous approval to Mayor Penfield Tate and Councilman Tim Fuller to proceed with a lawsuit against the city to allow them to be listed on the ballots with other candidates in the recall election. The District Court was to decide on this issue at the same trial challenging the petitions. That decision in late May was that the recall petitions were invalid because they did not contain sufficient grounds to force an election. The City Council then authorized an appeal of the decision to the Colorado Supreme Court. In August that Court overturned the District Court ruling and declared that the recall petitions were valid, and that Mayor Tate and Councilman Fuller could not run as successor candidates on the recall ballot.

In a "Camera" article dated September 1, Barry Koltnow reported that there was no evidence of a liberal bloc on Council. Of 184 votes recorded during the first eight months of 1974, Council voted unanimously on 142 occasions, or about 77 per cent of the time. The legendary 5-4 split occurred only five times ... and on three of those votes, conservative Walter Slack voted the same way as Councilman Fuller or Tate.

#### CITY MANAGER

In January, Barbara Morrison was named Boulder's new Transportation Director. James Murray, Director of Administrative Services, announced his resignation in April. In May, the City Manager announced the dismissal of the Director of Real Estate Services.

In early April, City Manager Twitchell said that the city would serve utilities to permit expansion of the Gunbarrel Shopping Center. This was a part of the strategy to block a proposed incorporation and development of a regional shopping center at Niwot.

The City Manager, in a June interview with Barry Koltnow for the "Camera", said, "Between the sexual orientation ordinance and the ensuing recall drive, a significant schism in the community resulted which prevented me from implementing my plan to introduce uniting issues." Comments about Mr. Twitchell came from some Council members. Mr. Slack said that he had been balanced in his approach to Council and had been willing to rethink past policies. Tim Fuller conceded that the City Manager had "bent over to give the council as much reins as it wanted to take." Mr. Twitchell commented that he wanted the council to be strong politically. "Leadership has got to come from political leaders, not from the city manager."

In November, the City Manager recommended that the city turn down an offer by the Public Service Company of \$900,000 to take over the bus system. The city had valued the franchise at \$3.5 million. The Public Service Company was obligated under the franchise to provide bus service to the city until 1990.

#### CITY ATTORNEY

In April, Mary Grumbine was appointed Assistant City Attorney, the first woman to serve in that position.

In October, the City Attorney's office said that it would recommend to City Council that it appeal the decision by the District Court denying the city's motion for a new trial in the Robinson case.

In December, the City Attorney was presenting oral arguments in two cases before the Colorado Supreme Court. One was on the Robinson case. The other was initiated by the city to clear up the question of how the mall affects the property rights of downtown property owners. In June, the District Court had ruled in favor of the city's mall position. The defendants, downtown businessmen, had appealed that ruling to the Supreme Court.

### ELECTIONS

The two elections of 1974 in which Boulder citizens voted on local issues were on May 7 at a special election on sexual preference, and on September 10 at a recall and Council election placed on a paper ballot available at the Primary Election.

In the May election, the voters turned down the sexual orientation ordinance by 64 per cent; 13,107 against to 7,438 for. According to a "Daily Camera" article dated May 8 written by Barry Koltnow:

The entire controversy began on December 18, 1973, when Council unanimously approved on first reading the ordinance banning job discrimination on the basis of sexual orientation. Postponements were granted on final action and several public hearings were held. A recall group formed in early February and threatened to recall all council members who voted in favor of the ordinance.

The tale continues in a "Colorado Daily" article written by Timothy Lange on October 31, 1975:

(On second reading), the council finally voted 5-4 in favor of the amendment (to the City's Human Rights ordinance), but agreed to put the issue on the ballot for the people to decide. By then, however, a recall effort had begun. ... A City Charter provision would not allow the recall of the three newly elected women who voted for the amendment, but Tate and Fuller were vulnerable. ... The recallers ... gathered 9600 signatures against Tate and Fuller in the most successful petition campaign in city history.

The two councilmen went to court arguing that the Charter language on recall elections was unfair. However, the Colorado Supreme Court ruled in favor of the Charter, and the election was held. An article in the "Daily Camera" of September 11 reported that George Boland was the highest vote-getter among the six successor candidates with 29 per cent of the total vote ... and would fill out the remaining 16 months of Fuller's four-year term. Timothy Fuller lost by a tally of 10,052 to 11,373. Penfield Tate held his council seat on a vote of 10,909 to 10,342. Boland ... received 8,972 votes out of 30,709 cast for the successors.

Although the sexual preference ordinance was the reason for the recall petition, it apparently was not the sole motivation of those who voted against Fuller, since it was Tate who introduced the ordinance. Tate and Fuller both theorized that the controversy surrounding Fuller's residency at the time of the 1971 election helped turn the voters against the councilman. ... There was some question at that time whether Fuller was a resident of Boulder for three years before his election to office. The Colorado Supreme Court eventually ruled that the three-year residency requirement was unconstitutional and the controversy died down.





## CHAPTER 11

## HEALTH AND SANITATION

As Boulder emerged from its cocoon stage and began to grow rapidly, the primary concern of the city government was how to supply its citizens with water and how to dispose of their wastes.

1965

### WATER SUPPLY AND SEWAGE DISPOSAL

Thanks to the foresight of officials and the people who voted to become a part of the Big Thompson Project in the 1950's, city water supplies in 1965 were adequate for a population of 80,000 according to Charles V. Hallenbeck, Jr., Boulder's water consultant. Through exchange with ditch users, Big Thompson water provided almost 25 per cent of the total amount consumed in Boulder in 1965.

Boulder voters had also taxed themselves to provide for sewage disposal. However, because of rapid growth and more and more stringent governmental regulations to protect the health of downstream users, the treatment plant, in operation since 1957, proved inadequate by 1965. The city had been notified three years earlier that its sewage effluent was violating pollution laws. A sewer bond issue put before the voters in March had failed. In June, the city was requested to appear before the State Board of Health to explain why improperly treated sewage was still being dumped into Boulder Creek. The City Attorney requested a delay until

Boulder could build an additional treatment plant and improve the old one. A second election on the issue was successful in July.

In February, sewer service had been granted to a development in Sunshine Canyon. It was the first time a sewer line had been extended above the "Blue Line". The "Blue Line" amendment to the City Charter (to restrict water service by the city only to that land lying below a contour line at essentially 5,750 feet in elevation) was adopted by the voters at a special election in July 1959 after being placed on the ballot by initiated petition. It was amended in a January 1961 election to allow water service to the National Center for Atmospheric Research.

In June, the Council had adopted a policy called the Service Area Concept, earlier known as the "Spokes of the Wheel" plan. This was an explanation of the reasons and the requirements for providing city water and sewer services to outlying areas. Since 1959, Boulder had extended service lines on a revocable permit basis only to nearby areas where a lack of city utilities might cause health or "orderly growth" problems for the city proper.

Also passed was an ordinance finalizing a water contract serving the new International Business Machines Corporation (IBM) structure. Terms of that contract required annexation of the property as soon as it became eligible (with one-sixth of its area contiguous to the city), payment of double the in-city water rate, plant investment fees one and one-half times as much as those charged in-city, and compliance with the city building regulations. These requirements were in line with the Service Area policy. The plant investment fee (PIF) charged at the time of hook-up reflected the philosophy that new growth should pay a substantial part of its own way by "buying in" to existing utility systems and gave the older residents of

the city a break in the seemingly unending burden of bond issues. This new philosophy was one result of pressures from the north where IBM was under construction, and from the south where some developers were planning a large subdivision above the "Blue Line" in the foothills near the National Center for Atmospheric Research. Even farther south of the city, Mr. Sam Rudd wanted to create a 160-acre sub-community. The Council had already agreed to extend another "spoke" of the utility wheel to the Rudd property when city residents petitioned for an election to decide whether water should be supplied to the area.

Because of the controls exercised by the city through its utilities, there had been an upsurge in the number of special utility districts which were claiming they could provide less expensive services. City Manager E. Robert Turner opposed the formation of private utilities in the fringe area because ... "it would forever preclude any opportunity for the city to include commercial and industrial tax values within the city." At his last Council meeting in July, Mr. Turner asked the Council to consider authorizing a study to find out how water could be provided for 125,000 people. This was after the election at which the voters had defeated the proposal to extend water south of the city.

#### REFUSE DISPOSAL

Another problem that Council had to wrestle with in 1965 was that of refuse disposal. In March, the voters had defeated a proposal for a city-wide franchise collection service. As a result of that defeat, backyard incinerators remained legal. Estimates prior to that election put the amount of trash burned in Boulder each year at 500 pounds per person, one cause of an increasing air pollution problem. During the campaign for the franchise plan, a question of equity had been raised. For 15 years,

the city had paid for garbage collection, largely limited to residents in the older sections of the community, plus boarding and student houses, hotels and restaurants. About 2000 addresses were served at city expense, but those not served had to help pay the bill. After the election, the Council extended its garbage contract for one year and determined to study the advisability of continuing the service.

Rates were raised in 1965 at the new Boulder County land-fill facility near Marshall Lake and the managers of the dump, the Rich-Land Company, started operation of a new compost plant.

1966

### WATER SUPPLY AND SEWAGE DISPOSAL

One year after adopting the Service Area Concept, the Council discussed amending it. It had proven workable for extending service to large, undeveloped areas such as the IBM plant, but the high initial cost of extending utility lines had been a stumbling block for areas where ownership was diversified and development had already taken place.

Such an area was the Wagoner Manor subdivision which needed immediate relief from a worsening situation. Wagoner depended on a series of shallow community wells for its water supply. Continued growth in the valley, which reduced the amount of water absorbed by the ground, was one cause for the depletion of the subsurface supply. A second area to the east was having difficulty because the proliferation of septic tanks had become so extensive that raw sewage was seeping into borrow pits along the roads and was contaminating some wells. Baseline Heights Water and Sanitation District had never been legally organized to provide sewer service. Its residents were paying nine mills to retire water bonds. The projected costs

of plant investment fees plus the levy to become a part of the city system seemed to be less economical although more healthful.

In September, the Council, already committed to provide utilities to the IBM site, agreed to a contract which stated that Boulder would operate and maintain the Boulder Valley Water and Sanitation District's sewer lines and package treatment plant until the city's new sewage disposal facility was completed. A site for that facility, two miles east of the "old" plant, was chosen in October. An alternate location even farther east was offered as a donation to the city, but the cost of extending lines more than offset any savings.

The Council, while awaiting an engineering report on expansion of the water system, and attempting to find solutions to sanitation problems plaguing nearby subdivisions, was under attack from some residents north of the city who believed their needs had been neglected. One promoter proposed the incorporation of a town of "North Boulder" saying the new city would have a firm option from a private utility to provide treated water. The High Mountain Waterline Company denied any such commitment. To combat this threat of uncontrolled sprawl promoted by the proliferation of special districts, the city paid the cost of oversizing the water main to the School District's Vocational-Technical Center on east Arapahoe, and prepared to do the same for a water extension along North Broadway to the intersection with 28th Street.

#### REFUSE DISPOSAL

A consultant on refuse disposal recommended that Boulder provide that service as a part of their public utilities. The city temporarily went into the trash collection business to forestall a potential health hazard from an accumulation of refuse at households and businesses whose haulers

had their licenses suspended for not using the County's disposal site. Boulder's haulers claimed the monopolistic aspects of the compost plant and the high dump fees were the bases for their protest. In December, the State Public Utilities Commission (PUC) denied applications by the haulers which would have allowed them to dump at any approved site within 30 miles of Boulder. PUC requirements were that Boulder-licensed haulers must dump their loads at city-designated locations within five miles of the city.

### HOUSING CODE

The Federal Housing Act of 1964 had stipulated certain requirements be met before a community could be eligible for grants. Among these was the enforcement of a housing code, an ordinance which Boulder had never passed. A Housing Code Review Committee was established by Council in August as a first step towards meeting this goal.

1967

### WATER SUPPLY AND SEWAGE DISPOSAL

The long-range water plans to accommodate the expected population increase in the valley were almost complete by 1967. The construction of a filtration plant at the Boulder Reservoir was given top priority so that western slope water could be used directly rather than by trading it as irrigation water with those who held direct flow rights to Boulder Creek water.

The city was making some progress in solving problems in the Service Area. This was the year that Council approved a sewer service contract with the Baseline Heights District.

#### REFUSE DISPOSAL

In April, the City Council accepted an administrative proposal to become involved in the compost operation and agreed to a pact with the trash haulers and the compost plant operators. The volume of refuse would increase because the county, under pressure from the state, adopted air pollution control measures that included a ban on backyard burning scheduled to go into effect in January 1968.

### HOUSING CODE

The Housing Code Review Committee determined that Boulder's Housing Code should be based on the American Public Health Association Code.

1968

### WATER SUPPLY AND SEWAGE DISPOSAL

In 1968, the Council authorized the execution of a contract providing for cooperation between Boulder and five other cities in northern Colorado for the development of a portion of the Big Thompson Reclamation Project to be known as the Windy Gap Project. They also voted to accept a 35-year contract with the Public Service Company under which the city would double the amount of its water storage capacity in Barker Reservoir near Nederland. An earlier water storage agreement had been established in 1955. The \$1.4 million contract was settled July 23 after two years of negotiations.

Also authorized was an agreement with the East Boulder Sanitation District providing for payments to the city in exchange for sewage treatment. The new 75th Street plant had opened in April.

#### REFUSE DISPOSAL

The Public Works Department had been receiving a rash of complaints about private trash haulers. The city ordinance governing trash collection regulated only the safety of the trucks on public roads and required that they be covered to prevent blowing trash. Open burning was now prohibited in the city and the county.

#### HOUSING CODE

The city had more power to improve or weed out dangerous or sub-standard housing after the new Housing Code was approved. The code was designed to upgrade dwellings which were considered dangerous, unsafe, unhygienic or unsanitary. It was estimated that about 2,000 units, many more than fifty years old, would violate the code in some manner.

1969

### WATER SUPPLY AND SEWAGE DISPOSAL

In 1969, the Council voted for the formation of a subdistrict of the Northern Colorado Water Conservancy District by the Six Cities Water Committee. The cities of Boulder, Estes Park, Fort Collins, Greeley, Longmont, and Loveland had been meeting to plan the development of new raw water supplies by constructing a reservoir at the confluence of the Colorado and Fraser Rivers. They had filed for Colorado River rights in 1967 for the Windy Gap Project.

An ordinance was adopted that required people seeking to supplement their private water supply with city water either arrange to sell their water rights to the city or to pay a higher rate for any city water they

might use.

A year after it opened, the sewage treatment plant was damaged by double explosions. Ernest Hamilton, Superintendent of the Sewer Utility, said that officials believed the cause had been a highly volatile chemical dumped into the system.

1970

### WATER SUPPLY AND SEWAGE DISPOSAL

In 1970, engineering consultants estimated the cost of the first two phases of the Six Cities proposal for the Windy Gap Reservoir at \$11 million.

Inflation was affecting the proposed addition to the sewer treatment facilities on 75th Street. The addition would allow it to serve an additional 80,000 people. In November the city was implementing a 50 per cent increase in its sewer service charges to finance the \$1.6 million improvement.

1971

### WATER SUPPLY AND SEWAGE DISPOSAL

In September, Boulder's new water plant at Boulder Reservoir began providing treated water to the system.

The city received a federal grant of \$528,000 which funded about one-third of the sewer plant expansion. Some citizens had urged a smaller project, indicating that anything larger might be an inducement for the city to allow more growth. City Manager Ted Tedesco replied that limiting growth mechanically was not the way to go. Outside the city limits, it was

now costing \$1,575 to tap the city sewer and water lines. Builders contended that Boulder had found the key it had been searching for to lock out new growth.

1972

### WATER SUPPLY AND SEWAGE DISPOSAL

In addition to its Six Cities Project, Boulder had attempted to acquire greater storage facilities for its historical water source in the mountains. In 1970, the city had purchased a 100-acre site and had planned a \$1 million Park Reservoir Project. By 1972, the plan had to be postponed because of inflation, lack of progress in use-permit talks with the United States Forest Service, and other factors including the concern of environmentalists about the size and impact of the project.

In 1972, one builder formally expressed his views in a District Court suit against the city, charging that denial of his request for city utility extension to his proposed subdivision was discriminatory. The Council had voted five to two to withhold water and sewer services from Larry Robinson, primarily because his development was not consistent with the interim growth policy adopted after the November 1971 election.

### AIR POLLUTION

In August, the Council approved city controls on vehicle exhaust emissions. And in November, Jim Adams, Boulder's Environmental Protection Officer, started educating the public and asking for cooperation in overcoming automobile pollution.

1973

### WATER SUPPLY AND SEWAGE DISPOSAL

In June 1973, Boulder officials acknowledged that the city's treatment plants were again dumping substandard effluent into Boulder Creek. This they attributed to conditions during the expansion program necessitated by rapid growth. The addition to the 75th Street plant was completed and tripled its capacity so it could serve a population of 120,000. In July, the Council voted to fund a feasibility study of the land application of sewage effluent. The State Health Department had been reluctant to approve land application because of the variability of soil types and water tables. There had also been legal questions about the secondary use of water without returning it to stream users, and whether the same standards on pollution would apply to land application as to stream pollution.

#### REFUSE DISPOSAL

The Council approved a request by the local landfill operator to convert the method of charging commercial haulers from a volume to a weight system. The operator said the rate of return on his investment had been just 1.1 per cent since taking over the landfill in 1968.

1974

### WATER SUPPLY AND SEWAGE DISPOSAL

By 1974, the Windy Gap Project had progressed to the public hearing stage. An estimated \$2 million in bonds would be needed initially to cover design and engineering costs. The city's Public Facilities Director,

Robert Westdyke, favored Windy Gap because it would not require the construction of complete facilities, since the existing Colorado-Big Thompson Project equipment could be utilized. Also, the water could be eligible for re-use by the city after being run through the water and sewer treatment facilities. In July, the Council unanimously approved a one-year commitment of study funds for the project, which was under fire from western slope appropriators.

In May, Judge William Neighbors ruled that the city had created a monopoly on water and sewer services and had thus become a public utility. He ordered the city to extend utilities to the property of Larry Robinson.

Also in May, the Council authorized the City Manager to provide water for the next 60 days to the Wagoner Water District which again was in an emergency situation because its wells were drying up.

The Council received final reports on wastewater from two consulting firms. Bauer Engineering recommended using federal funds and a portion of Boulder's "Open Space" sales tax revenue to implement land application of sewage effluent. The second firm, CH2M Hill suggested several alternatives, but recommended delaying any land application program. One alternative was an advanced treatment plant that, within three to five years, would clean Boulder's effluent to standards.





## CHAPTER III

## TRAFFIC ENGINEERING AND TRANSPORTATION

Second only to health in Council priorities during the sixties was the problem of how to move the increasing number of people from place to place, and how to park them once they had arrived.

1965

### HIGHWAYS AND BYWAYS

In the fall of 1965, the results of a two-year study to determine ways of increasing the traffic-carrying capability of Broadway were being prepared for presentation to the Council; and a request for construction funds for a limited access by-pass east of Boulder had been sent to the State Highway Department.

### PARKING

Leasing parking lots became a method for a municipality to provide space immediately without the need for costly land acquisition. The leases called for the city to pay the owners a set per cent of net return on their investments which included the cost of the land, demolition of buildings and asphaltting the lots. The city paid to install and maintain the parking meters. Boulder had successfully arranged a lease on one lot at the northwest corner of Spruce Street and Broadway. Negotiations for two additional off-street lots, one on University Hill and the other at 15th and Pearl Streets, had been temporarily halted pending the outcome of a

lawsuit challenging the legality of a proposed lot at 1922 Broadway. The District Court ruled in favor of the city by granting a motion to dismiss the suit.

1966

### HIGHWAYS AND BYWAYS

In 1966, the Council unanimously passed a resolution declaring the need for a major arterial by-pass along 47th Street east of Boulder, and the state approved funds for engineering studies and right-of-way acquisition for the road.

The Council expressed interest in a one-way pair for Broadway and 13th Street.

They voted seven to one to proceed with the improvement of Baseline Road between 28th and 30th Streets. The one dissenting vote was because of the University's reluctance to pay as much for the project as the city had requested.

### PEDESTRIANS AND BIKEWAYS

The 1966 budget had earmarked \$10,000 for sidewalk improvements. Under the proposed program, the city would contract for the work and assess most costs against benefited properties. Owners would have as many as five years to pay their share. Pressure for sidewalk improvements had increased substantially because of litigation stemming from injuries suffered from sidewalk falls.

#### PARKING

Parking in downtown Boulder became free on Saturdays for the first time in 40 years. The action was taken at the request of Centroplex, an

organization of downtown merchants and property owners. On-street parking had been regulated in 1923 and metered parking started in Boulder in 1926, according to Police Chief Myron Teegarden.

#### TRANSIT

Boulder had joined the Metro Area Transportation Study in order to qualify for federal funds. All projects in urban areas had to be based on a comprehensive planning process in order to receive the money.

1967

### HIGHWAYS AND BYWAYS

At this time the 47th Street by-pass was scheduled for construction beginning in late 1968, and was to be built from Colorado 93 near Marshall to the Longmont Diagonal. The proposal called for a total of eight bridges at the interchange of the Turnpike with South Boulder Road.

Among the proposed major thoroughfare improvements that were scheduled to be funded by the increased sales tax revenues approved in November were: Arapahoe Avenue to become four-laned, cutting across Boulder Creek west of downtown to join Canyon Boulevard; Broadway and Iris scheduled for widening; and 24th Street (Folsom) for connection to South Broadway.

Early in the year, the Council had told the administration to move ahead on plans to revamp intersections along Broadway on University Hill, but to defer any decision on pairing Broadway and 13th Street as one-way traffic carriers through the downtown.

#### PARKING

After months of negotiations with Dr. Phillip P. Pyles, the city formalized a 25-year lease agreement to allow a city-operated parking

structure near 14th Street and College Avenue.

#### TRANSIT

Pavement of parking aprons and taxi-ways at the Boulder Airport was authorized. The city paid half the cost and the fixed-base operators paid the balance.

1968

### HIGHWAYS AND BYWAYS

Some property owners along Baseline Road between 28th and 30th Streets objected to their street assessment charges, claiming that their property had not been benefited by the project. The road had been in the county at the time of the improvements.

Preliminary plans for the widening to six lanes of Baseline between 30th and 47th Streets, and Pearl Street between 28th and 47th Streets were approved by Council.

Four years after a city ordinance created Street Improvement District 7A in east central Boulder, north from Pearl to Bluff and east from 21st to 28th Street, the work was finally scheduled to begin. Since 1964, the program had been delayed by court cases initiated by a resident of the area.

Bargaining with the State Highway Department continued over differences between their plans for the 47th Street freeway and the city's desire to upgrade those plans. Any variation was to be funded by Boulder. The state had designed a by-pass with diamond interchanges. The city wanted a traffic-way with cloverleaf interchanges. The state funded only design costs and more right-of-way acquisition in 1968.

### PARKING

Boulder residents continued to park free downtown so the city could continue a study of parking trends. In March, parking during rush hours was banned on both sides of Broadway between 20th Street and Canyon Boulevard. Later, that parking was eliminated entirely in order to increase the traffic-carrying capacity and to allow the street crews to remove snow and ice more easily.

1969

### HIGHWAYS AND BYWAYS

The Council authorized the purchase of 245 acres at 47th and Pearl Streets to provide the right-of-way for a partial cloverleaf interchange at that intersection when the 47th Street freeway was constructed.

Iris Avenue residents protested against a project to widen and improve their street. The Council's Assessment Equity Committee prepared a report on the necessity for a major thoroughfare link between Broadway and the Longmont Diagonal in north Boulder. In 1969, the two-lane roadway was adequate for 6,000 vehicles daily. The projected volume for 1989 on Iris was 11,000. The report also treated other questions raised by Iris area residents, including the project's effect on groundwater levels, administrative costs included in the assessments, and benefits which would accrue to properties from street improvements and from storm drainage facilities.

The long-delayed controversial 24th Street (Folsom) major thoroughfare project was established. The district included storm drainage improvements, left-turn bays, and paving from Boulder Creek to Pearl Street.

Kent Dewell, the city's Traffic Engineer, redesigned the University Hill traffic pattern. Plans included the widening of Broadway to four lanes and the installation of traffic signals at the intersection of Broadway and Pennsylvania Avenue.

#### PEDESTRIANS AND BIKEWAYS

In August 1969, an agreement was reached with the Boulder Valley School District so the city could extend a bicycle path five blocks from the Boulder High football field along Boulder Creek to 17th Street.

#### PARKING

The Council approved an ordinance which would permit the formation of general improvement districts within the city. Businessmen in downtown Boulder and on University Hill had requested the legislation to allow them to underwrite the construction of off-street parking areas.

#### TRANSIT

During 1969, the city was negotiating with the Public Service Company on the terms of their 20-year franchise. Sections of the agreement dealt with the company's use of Boulder's streets and alleys for its utility poles, the possibility and costs of undergrounding electric lines, and the responsibility for continuing bus transportation services.

Boulder was awarded a \$50,000 grant from the United States Department of Transportation for the city's urban mass transportation study.

### HIGHWAYS AND BYWAYS

In January 1970, the Council authorized condemnation of a parcel of property in the 900 block of Arapahoe Avenue. The land was needed for the widening of Arapahoe as proposed in the Boulder Tomorrow traffic circulation plan. The city administration was instructed to use major thoroughfare and open space sales tax funds for the purchase.

A tentative construction schedule was set for completion of the 47th Street freeway. The stretch between the Turnpike and Baseline Road should be done in about two years and that northward to the Diagonal in about five years. Residents of the area requested a delay in construction until the interchanges could be redesigned so they would still have access to the Table Mesa area.

Plans for the extension of 24th Street (renamed Folsom) through campus to Broadway were questioned by Colorado University officials because of heavy use of the street by pedestrians.

#### PEDESTRIANS AND BIKEWAYS

Federal funds were allocated to provide pedestrian and bicycle underpasses on 28th Street east of the University campus.

#### PARKING

In April, the Boulder Tomorrow Parking Committee prepared to mail out booklets explaining a new plan for downtown parking structures. The new plan would cost about \$1.3 million. It called for five new parking sites, one on two levels above the National State Bank parking lot and the Watts-Hardy Dairy lot at 13th and Walnut Streets, and surface parking at the other four sites, including the Hollister Motor Company land at 11th and

Walnut Streets and the area west of the Boulderado Hotel at Broadway and Spruce Street. Annual cost of the program would be about \$190,000, and would be met by revenues from the parking lots and assessments on the merchants by themselves. The Central Area Improvement District was set up by ordinance on August 18, 1970. Provision was made for the District to incur \$1,400,000 in general obligation indebtedness for the purpose of providing funds for acquisition of land and the construction of parking and off-street parking facilities. A contract was negotiated for one site on Walnut between 11th Street and Broadway for \$210,000.

#### TRANSIT

The Public Service Company's 20-year franchise with the city was approved in the August election. Part of the agreement stated that the Company would continue to furnish public transportation in the city; but if losses to the Company continued after five years, the city would defray one-half the actual losses incurred. That part of the franchise was left open for renegotiation at any time.

1971

### HIGHWAYS AND BYWAYS

In January, the Council approved the widening of 30th Street despite the lack of cooperation by the owners of the Crossroads Shopping Center. The project included four-laning from Arapahoe Avenue to the Longmont Diagonal, intersection left-turn bays, an extensive drainage system, sidewalks, and the undergrounding of telephone lines. Left-turn bays into the Crossroads Center were eliminated from the plans after Council was told the land needed would have to be obtained through condemnation. In March,

Gerrit Von Frellick, a part owner of Crossroads, told the city he would ask the District Court for a preliminary injunction against the formation of the 30th Street Improvement District. His primary objection was the suggested plan for a future one-way pairing of 28th and 30th Streets. The suit was dismissed in June, but an appeal was set in motion. Mr. Von Frellick sold his interest in the property, and the new owners announced they would not appeal the dismissal. The new management also agreed to dedicate land and pay added costs to allow left-turn bays from 30th Street into the Center.

The Council approved a \$30,000 state-federally funded project to increase the traffic-carrying capacity at 28th Street and Colorado Avenue.

The Transportation Department proposed a Broadway-14th Street one-way system to improve north-south traffic flow.

In April, by a one-vote margin, the Council voted to "eat" a \$75,000 assessment on the Iris Avenue thoroughfare project, thus removing the tax burden from residents of that area. The city was now responsible for the total cost and Council debated whether it had set a precedent for future major thoroughfare projects.

#### PEDESTRIANS AND BIKEWAYS

In another policy change, Boulder citizens who qualified as low-income homeowners could defer street and sidewalk assessments which could now become liens on their property at time of resale.

In May, an estimated 2,500 people held a "Bicycles for Boulder" rally. Mayor Robert Knecht rode with them and told the group that the city had appropriated some \$20,000 for bikeways in Boulder, about three times as much as in 1970.

#### PARKING

In January, Council had approved the sale of \$700,000 in bonds to help finance Boulder's downtown off-street parking plan. The first site contracted for was the Hollister Motor property at 11th and Walnut Streets. Plans for another downtown site at 15th and Walnut Streets were referred back to the Parking District's advisory committee because the city did not want to be responsible for tearing down the historic 99-year-old Central School building located on the property.

The 40-space University Hill off-street parking lot was opened. Priced at about \$162,000, its bonds were to be re-financed from half the revenue from Hill area parking meters.

#### TRANSIT

In November, the Council appointed nine persons to an advisory committee that would suggest alternatives for public transportation planning. Of immediate concern were the on-going negotiations between the city and the Public Service Company about improving the bus system.

1972

### HIGHWAYS AND BYWAYS

At a meeting in March, Councilman Penfield Tate's compromise resolution on 47th Street passed six to two. The language of the statement denoted the need for an added, high-capacity north-south limited access parkway for Boulder, but stressed that it be aesthetically pleasing and environmentally sound. Transportation Director Kent Dewell reported that the state had agreed to set a 45 mile per hour speed limit, to drop plans for a concrete median barrier, and to forego the southerly extension to South Foothills Highway until it was requested by the city.

In May, the financing package for the \$1.14 million 30th Street project was approved. Under the formula, the city would pay \$775,000 and the property owners between Arapahoe and the Longmont Diagonal would pay \$360,000. The Transportation Director said that he had applied for federal funding for the project, but that it had not met grant specifications.

#### PEDESTRIANS AND BIKEWAYS

The Council had decided to change the bicycle-licensing fee from a one-time, 50 cent charge to an annual fee of \$3.00. The city expected this to net revenue of about \$64,000 a year towards construction of a proposed 70 miles of bikeways.

#### PARKING

In October, a budget of \$852,122 was approved for the Central Area Parking District, including for the first time the levy of a 13-mill property tax within the assessment district.

#### TRANSIT

The first total transportation study in Boulder's history was presented to the City Council in March by the Ken R. White Company. The \$162,000 study advocated a people-mover system for the Boulder Valley that would carry 30 per cent of the area's travel load by 1995. In July, the Citizen's Advisory Committee on Public Transportation submitted its report. The Committee commended the Ken R. White report proposing a five-route bus system supplemented by dial-a-buses and a 22-mile fixed track personal rapid transit mode. First year costs of the system were estimated at \$2.47 million which would be supported by fares, a wheel tax, an emissions tax, a parking tax, and Public Service Company and city subsidies. Some of these financing suggestions were termed "disincentives" to the use of the

automobile.

In August the Council was requested to defer any local vote on city acquisition of the bus system until the election in the fall of 1973 which would determine participation in the Regional Transportation District (RTD). James Bowers, Boulder's RTD Board member, said that they were studying three levels of a regional transit system, all incorporating local and inter-city bus systems plus a fixed-rail system.

City Attorney Walt Wagenhals was requested to draft an ordinance setting a special election on "un-earmarking" the for-streets-only major thoroughfare fund. The election, sought by Councilman Kenneth Wright, could allow some of the sales tax revenues to be used for mass transit.

1973

### HIGHWAYS AND BYWAYS

The 47th Street Improvement Association filed a suit in Boulder District Court seeking to halt federal funding for the project by demonstrating it was approved in violation of the Environmental Policy Act. They requested that the funding, amounting to 55 per cent of the total, be held up until impact statements were prepared. In May, Council voted to recommend the deletion of the 47th Street extension south of the Turnpike from the Boulder Valley Comprehensive Plan. The County Commissioners also had to agree to the change. In July, the State Highway Department announced that it had downgraded the project from a major freeway to a parkway, thus lowering the speed limit and increasing landscaping and noise controls.

The Council agreed that the conversion of 28th and 30th Streets to a one-way system should have priority because of traffic flow problems and accident statistics. There were some reservations expressed because of the

probability of increased traffic in front of the Williams Village dormitories if 30th Street were opened to north-bound cars coming off the Turnpike, and because of potential losses to businesses on both streets if access were from only one direction.

The Colorado University Board of Regents approved a plan for the partial closing of Folsom and 18th Streets on campus. The Planning Board reversed an earlier decision to depress Folsom, and voted six to one in favor of the University's proposal to close it and re-route traffic to Regent Drive.

The Board voted unanimously to drop a request for a feasibility study for routing Canyon Boulevard under Crossroads Shopping Center, and voted six to one to delete the widening of Arapahoe from Folsom westward from any future transportation plans.

In a March article by "Daily Camera" reporter Barry Koltnow, it was announced that Boulder's unique \$150,000 computerized traffic control system would soon be operational, many months behind schedule. "The TRW system is the latest in advanced traffic signal technology and is in operation in only five other cities in the country." A later article proclaimed that the control system would be put into effect along Broadway and 28th Street for a trial period in August.

Boulder paved one block, on 14th Street from Walnut to Canyon (the old railroad depot site) with "glassphalt". In 1972, former City Engineer Sam Maphis and Eco-Center Director Sandy Cooper joined in a mass collection drive that netted some 30 tons of glass. The material was given to Flatirons Paving Company to crush and mix with a paving substance.

#### PEDESTRIANS AND BIKEWAYS

In a report to Council in April, Dave Whitlow, Administrative Assistant to the City Manager, told of the problems of collecting the bicycle license fees. Of the expected \$10,000 in revenues, only \$500 had been received by April 15th. In October, the bicycle-licensing program was completely revamped. The goal was no longer to get revenue, but to protect from theft. The annual \$3.00 fee was changed to cover the life of ownership of the bike.

The opening of the Broadway bikeway was celebrated by more than 100 enthusiasts riding the six-mile route. Budgeted for construction in 1974 were 16.5 miles of bicycle paths.

#### PARKING

The downtown Parking District decided not to pursue a proposal to construct a \$1.3 million underground parking garage on Walnut Street between Broadway and 13th Street. In July 1971, the estimated cost had been \$954,000. In a three-party agreement with the First National and the National State Banks, the District's 1973 share was to have been a little over \$600,000.

In December, an ordinance was passed to prohibit downtown workers from keeping their cars in the same space all day by feeding the meter every two hours.

#### TRANSIT

The proposal to ask Boulder voters to reconsider their 1967 decision to earmark sales tax revenue for construction of major thoroughfares was scheduled to be placed on the November ballot. If approved, it would allow expenditures of those funds for transportation. City Manager Ted Tedesco

was strongly opposed to the suggestion, saying that thoroughfare plans for the next five to seven years would be jeopardized. He contended that the Boulder transportation system would be rubber-tired and would need those thoroughfares.

In a "Camera" article dated May 9, Barry Koltnow reported:

Twice in the last two months, Acting City Manager Bill Lamont and Jack Rouse, Manager of Public Service Company's Boulder division have announced a "final contract agreement" only to have those agreements break down. Now the document has been signed and the bus system will expand to five routes but will retain its present 30-minute frequency. In August, the frequencies will increase to 15 minutes during peak periods. The contract, which will expire January 1976, calls for a subsidy of \$425,000 a year from Public Service Company and \$115,000 a year from the city.

In September, Councilman Timothy Fuller proposed a bus fare subsidization program to provide free bus fare for the elderly, children under 16 years old and for all families at the poverty level.

1974

### HIGHWAYS AND BYWAYS

In April, the proposed one-way couplet on 28th and 30th Streets was approved and budgeted for \$1.2 million. The system would no longer extend south of Colorado Avenue, but would have a connecting loop on land south of Boulder Creek. The design phase of the project was delayed to allow time to study its social, economic and environmental impacts. The City Council requested a court opinion on whether the city should pay for land for rights-of-way at the before- or after-development value.

In the downtown area, a Pearl-Spruce one-way system was approved as a major east-west arterial, but Pearl Street had been suggested frequently as the possible site of a pedestrian mall. A one-way north-south couplet for 13th and 14th Streets took priority at this time. Pine Street would be the

connecting link on the north, and right angle turns would be used rather than demolishing houses to provide gentler transitions.

City Transportation Director Barbara Morrison asked that a plan to widen Arapahoe Avenue to eight lanes be stricken from the Boulder Valley Comprehensive Plan. Land owners were loath to invest in that area for fear of future condemnation of their property for right-of-way.

A mile-long section of the controversial 47th Street (Foothills Parkway) opened with much of the landscaping completed. Some of the controversy was generated by the fear that the parkway would become a link in a planned inter-state highway system west of Denver.

In August, the Council unanimously approved a resolution establishing a pedestrian mall. According to Barry Koltnow of the "Daily Camera":

... construction on the mall could begin by spring, 1975, ... . It is expected to take three years, however, to complete the one-way couplets on Broadway and 14th Street and on Pearl and Spruce. ... about 60 per cent of the \$5.5 million price tag on the mall will go toward construction of the two couplets. That construction will be financed through the city's major thoroughfare fund.

In September 1971, the state and the city had been funded through the federal TOPICS program to install in Boulder a computerized signalization system on 28th Street and on Broadway. The result had been a "research and development" project. Acceptance of the system as being completely installed did not occur until April 1974. Until that time, the system had never worked for more than five consecutive days.

The Transportation Director increased the 1974 allocation for Boulder's street maintenance and overlay programs. Run on a cycle, the plan was supposed to overlay streets every seven years and the city was running about four years behind schedule.

The residents of the University Heights area won a battle with the city over a median that had been installed between Folsom and 28th Streets

on Colorado Avenue. Construction of the median had prevented them from making left turns to the north side of that avenue. Under pressure, the city agreed to remove the barrier except for the first 200 feet near the Folsom and Colorado intersection.

#### PEDESTRIANS AND BIKEWAYS

Barry Koltnow wrote in a "Camera" article on April 30 that, "The city will soon begin painting the town with bikeways. The allocation of \$100,000 this year has gone primarily into the University project along Broadway."

Since the experimental closing of 18th Street and Colorado through campus, the section of Folsom between Colorado and Regent Drive had been heavily impacted, endangering pedestrians and bicyclists. Students wrote to the University and to the city requesting relief.

#### PARKING

In January, the Council had tabled a proposed purchase of land on Broadway between Walnut Street and the Public Service Company building. The land, occupied by seven specialty shops, would have added 32 more spaces to an adjacent parking lot. In July, Council gave approval for the city to demolish the Boulder Mattress Factory at the corner of 14th Street and Canyon Boulevard and replace it with a 40 space parking lot. The lot was to be considered temporary because a performing arts center was planned for that site.

#### TRANSIT

The Regional Transportation District Act became effective July 1, 1969. Organizational planning and local governmental appointments (20 appointed by county commissioners with approval from the municipalities,

and a 21st elected at large) were completed by February 1970. In early 1971, the Regional Transportation District (RTD) selected a joint venture team of Development Research Associates and Wallace, McHarg, Roberts and Todd. In January 1974, the RTD was in the process of negotiating contracts with all municipalities within the District that were currently providing bus services. The Boulder Council had to decide whether to maintain the status quo with the Public Service Company (PSC) running the system until January 1976, or to acquire the bus system and withdraw from the RTD, or to contract for bus service with the RTD. In February, the Council accepted in principle the eventual inclusion of local buses under the District.

Ken Frizell reported in the "Camera" in November that the RTD Board of Directors had approved a contract whereby the city would turn over its share of the Boulder bus system to the District for \$397,500.

City Manager Archie Twitchell was authorized to seek from the Public Service Company some alternate form of service to the city in lieu of the \$400,000 they had been pumping into the bus system. Negotiations between the city administration and the PSC failed. At one point the city suggested undergrounding of power lines as an "alternative benefit" equal to the operation of the bus system. That was unacceptable to the PSC, which protested that there was no "alternative benefit" clause in the franchise. Instead they offered the city \$924,000 including the bus system. The Council turned down that offer and made a counter proposal of \$2.5 million, a figure the Company flatly refused to consider.





## CHAPTER IV

## LAND USE

Concurrent with health and traffic plans, land use regulations were being updated. A master plan for the Boulder area had been developed in the late 1950's and revised in the early 1960's. A single-sheet map with text on its reverse side called the "Guide for Growth" attempted to forecast future land patterns in and near the city so public facility needs would be more predictable.

1965

### PLANNING AND ZONING

In April, a rezoning request for 20 acres on the south side of Boulder Creek between 28th and 30th Streets was denied by Council. Residents of the East Ridge and the Cordry subdivisions questioned the advisability of transitional zoning in their area and any subsequent traffic and flood problems. Some suggested the new classification of planned development might protect their homes.

In July, plans for a 925 acre subdivision south of Bear Creek and above the "Blue Line" were presented to the Planning Board. Named Mountain Shadows, the proposed development would have 555 residential lots of an acre or more. It was to be served by a water system relying on two wells, and planned to have individual septic tanks.

At a September Planning Board meeting on the 1964 Hill Area Study a petition signed by 43 "Hill" merchants opposing expansion of business zoning was presented. Despite the opposition of the Planning Board and the

merchants, the Council later passed a resolution designating possible areas for extension by making the Hill Study a part of the "Guide for Growth".

In November, the Council unanimously approved the idea of selling four acres of the Pow Wow grounds to the YMCA for a proposed building site. The land had been given to the city by the Pow Wow Association.

### SHOPPING CENTERS

The Colorado Land and Investment Company had applied to the county for business zoning to permit a 23 acre shopping center near the intersection of 28th Street and North Broadway. Under a policy of cooperation, the County Planning Commission had asked the city Planning Board for recommendations, since the area was near the city limits. The city planning staff made a study and suggested there be no commercial use of the site. Following that recommendation, the City Council opposed the shopping center development. In December 1965, the County Planning Commission had unanimously defeated the rezoning request.

1966

### PLANNING AND ZONING

In addition to the threat of large subdivisions served by special districts which might weaken the city's control over its future development, there was also the danger of new incorporation within its sphere of influence.

In January, a meeting was held in north Boulder, presided over by the president of the Colorado Land and Investment Company, to discuss development of the area. It was attended by City Manager Robert W. Quinlan and his Administrative Assistant, Archie Twitchell, and City Attorney Peter Dietze. They were requested to leave. The meeting participants planned a petition

for incorporation of a new town to be named "North Boulder". Its boundaries would start generally at Jay Road and Sumac and extend three-and-one-half miles north. It would include between 9,000 and 12,000 acres and have an estimated population of 600.

In March, Governor John Love signed a bill which prohibited the incorporation of a new town of less than 320 acres and less than 100 persons per square mile within a mile of the border of an established community. The "North Boulder" move was thrown out of District Court on its ruling that the petition for an incorporation election was insufficient.

Planning Director James Bowers gave his first presentation of a slide show on "Boulder: Past, Present, and Future" in March. He stated that downtown Boulder, no longer the geographical center of area population, should be used for specialty shops, offices, and for cultural purposes. He gave five alternatives for managing growth ranging from stopping it within the city to creating sub-communities in the valley.

The Council later authorized a \$5,000 increase in the planning budget to provide for the preparation of a new comprehensive plan, or guide for growth, for Boulder to replace the one adopted ten years before.

In May, the Council adopted a new zoning ordinance intended to guide an orderly transition from rural to urban uses. The agricultural district allowed only one dwelling on five acres. Permitted uses included nurseries and fish hatcheries.

In July, the first subdivision to follow planned residential development procedure was presented by George Writer for the area southeast of the National Center for Atmospheric Research. Previous subdivisions had had to meet minimum lot size requirements. Under the new plan, lot sizes

could be smaller, but must be offset by larger areas of green space within the development.

Part of the historic Maxwell Ranch was being developed into an exclusive residential area named Spring Valley Estates. The new area had its own water supply pumped from two deep wells.

In August, the Council voted to recommend that the county join the Inter-County Regional Planning Commission and that Boulder become a participating municipality in the Commission. County and city membership were newly instituted requirements for receiving federal funds.

At a Council meeting in September, the problem of differences between city and county codes was discussed. The county building code applied only to recorded subdivisions. City sign code provisions were more restrictive than those in the county, which discouraged application for annexation and made it difficult for in-city businesses to compete with neighboring county merchants who could display larger signs.

#### DOWNTOWN

In April, Mayor Robert Knecht announced the formation of the Committee for the Exploration of the Core Area Potential (CECAP). This was not an official city group, but an association of groups interested in stimulating new thinking about the downtown area.

Among the speakers invited to their meetings was a partner in the firm of Victor Gruen Associates. By July, members of CECAP had agreed to work on creating a master plan for the central area. In September, the Committee ceased to exist. A non-profit Colorado corporation called Boulder Tomorrow took over its functions, and those who had served as officers of CECAP were elected officers of the new organization. Its president was Mayor Knecht, its vice-president was Joseph Stepanek, its

secretary, Ruth Wright, and its treasurer, Clyde Reedy. The city authorized a supplemental appropriation of \$15,000 to be given to Boulder Tomorrow.

1967

### PLANNING AND ZONING

By January 1967, the idea of creating a regional staff to pool planning resources had been endorsed by the County Regional Planning Commission and the City Planning Board. It called for a planning director to be named by a special council set up to oversee administration. The director, in turn, would name his staff. The function of the staff would be to carry out long-range planning, with short-range or specific planning problems to be determined by the existing planning boards, assisted by resident planners. City Council members saw the need for closer ties with the county and other political jurisdictions.

Boulder's new City Manager, Ted Tedesco, announced that the Boulder Valley Comprehensive Plan, slated for presentation to the Council in August, would be delayed indefinitely until the new city and county planning directors became familiar with its background. Some of the ideas considered for incorporation into the master plan, such as the concept of "sub-communities" and "open area reserves", had been suggested in a joint city-county report on "Boulder's Fringe Area Objectives" in 1964.

In zoning matters, the Council voted down a proposal by William J. Suitts for multi-family residential units on 85 acres at the southeast corner of Baseline Road and Thunderbird Drive. The 1957 "Guide for Growth" had shown high-density residential use of that land.

Plans for locating a Safeway store in Table Mesa were defeated in June

when the Council voted five to one to deny a request for planned development zoning of a site on the south side of Table Mesa Drive opposite Stanford Avenue. The decision centered on the amount of land desirable for business use in the area and the ways for providing a buffer between commercial and single family residential areas. In March, the County Commissioners had granted business zoning for a shopping center on the south side of Table Mesa Drive east of 46th Street against the recommendations of both the city and the county planning groups. There was some uneasiness expressed at a Council meeting that the Safeway store might relocate to that area, thus jeopardizing the southerly leg of the planned 47th Street by-pass.

In June, Council members voted unanimously to oppose the development of a subdivision on 30 acres on the northeast slope of Flagstaff Mountain. Reasons cited were excessive road grades, drainage problems with septic systems, run-off problems, and fire hazard. The Council was warned that the time was fast approaching when the city would have to pay more than lip service to proposals to buy open space land. In November, after passage of the sales tax measure, the city authorized its Attorney to proceed with court action to gain immediate possession of 17 acres of land on Flagstaff, and appropriated \$57,000 from the general fund to cover court expenses.

#### DOWNTOWN

Boulder Tomorrow had almost reached its initial financial goal by mid-January, and had received the City Council's approval to hire Victor Gruen Associates of Los Angeles as the consultant to develop the conceptual plan. The study area stretched from the base of the foothills to 21st Street, and from the bluffs of University Hill to the bases of Mapleton and Sunset Hills. In August, Edgardo Contini, a partner in the firm, presented

a plan for downtown Boulder which included a complex of offices, retail and governmental buildings interspersed with greenery and water, pedestrian malls and parking facilities.

1968

### PLANNING AND ZONING

Boulder was awarded a \$46,000 federal grant in February to help pay for a master plan. The comprehensive plan was far from being finalized. Ahead lay public hearings, conferences and more staff work. It was anticipated that its completion would set the stage for Boulder's growth.

More than 100 citizens, city officials and members of city boards, commissions, and civic groups drew up a set of proposed goals for Boulder in August. One assumption on which these goals was based was that Boulder would continue to grow, and energy must be devoted, not to prevent growth, but to affect its rate and to shape it to desired ends.

There were several bitter zoning battles during the year. In January, determined opposition of residents in the area delayed a rezoning at North Broadway and Juniper. John Hatch, the developer of the property, had come before Council with a proposal which would permit construction of a 22-unit apartment complex. Four months later the fight was resumed, with residents pleading for preservation of single-family dwellings on the west side of Broadway, while the city administration argued that the multi-family zoning proposal would be a logical transition between the commercial development on the east side of the street and the single-family homes to the west. The citizens won, as the ordinance was defeated in a tie vote by the City Council.

Another 110-acre parcel of land in north Boulder between 19th and 26th

Streets on both sides of Kalmia was subjected to zoning in March. It had been annexed to the city in December 1967 after heated discussion between those who desired to remain in a country-style way of life, and those who wanted smaller lots to make development feasible. With a minor amendment, the Council passed the zoning ordinance that made the bulk of the area one of single family residences on lots of 10,000 square feet.

A proposed Planned Unit Development (PUD) that would eventually cover 117 acres northwest of Boulder (Wonderland Hill) received enthusiastic initial acceptance from the Council in March. Approval of a PUD was a complicated affair involving two appearances before the Planning Board and two before the Council. After each agreement on a preliminary site plan, a final site plan had to win acceptance.

Another long-standing zoning squabble between the city and the owner of a large piece of property southeast of 27th Street and Baseline Road revolved around Boulder's plan to extend Moorhead Avenue to intersect with 27th Street. If this were done, it would cut directly through land owned by Bauldie Moschetti. The council voted to zone part of the area for business and part for multi-family residential use, hoping that the right-of-way dedication problems could be resolved later.

After a six-month study, several amendments to the zoning code were approved by Council in July. One provided for a more limited definition of rooming houses to reduce to three the number of unrelated persons allowable in a single-family home. Previous to this time, up to five unrelated persons could rent and live in such a dwelling.

The Board of Zoning Adjustment denied a request by Western Industries for a variance to permit construction of a 17-story office and apartment building at 26th Street and Canyon Boulevard. The structure was designed to be 220 feet high.

One major piece of legislation passed in 1968 was the culmination of three years of work and meetings with subdividers and developers. New subdivision regulations would replace a 17-year-old code adopted in 1951. One new requirement was that developers provide for a 50-year or a 100-year flood plain, that area estimated to be capable of accommodating water brought in by a flood of the size which could be expected every 50 or 100 years.

#### SHOPPING CENTERS

In February, a rezoning request to allow a large shopping center near 55th Street and the Longmont Diagonal was denied by the County Commissioners. Later in the year, there was an announcement of plans for a large shopping center in the Gunbarrel Greens area.

#### DOWNTOWN

The Council unanimously approved a detailed study and planning for Boulder Tomorrow's Concept Number Five designed by Victor Gruen Associates. Realization of the concept was many years in the future according to Mayor Robert W. Knecht. Funds for the study came from a federal grant, the city, and the Boulder Tomorrow group.

1969

### PLANNING AND ZONING

In September, the City and the County Planning Departments unveiled a plan for the future of the Boulder Valley for public comment. Essential to understanding the Boulder Valley Comprehensive Plan were the assumptions upon which it was based. To the extent that those assumptions were valid,

the plan would also be valid. That growth can be controlled in the valley was one major premise. Another was that no new industry of a size comparable to the IBM plant be planned, because the impact of services needed by its employees would be too much for governmental agencies to cope with.

This was the year of the battles about the power of the Board of Zoning Adjustment and the height of buildings. The city took steps in March towards the development of a high-rise luxury apartment building planned by architect James Hunter at Sixth Street and Canyon Boulevard. The Zoning Adjustment Board approval of the project was based on five hours of consideration, city ordinances, and some value judgement, according to its Chairman George Boland.

In May after several weeks of debate, the Council decided to impose a temporary limit of 100 feet on the height of all new buildings. Tentative approval was given in July for rezoning of property for the 100-foot-high Horizon West apartment building on 24th Street. In September, Western Industries Inc. filed a damage suit against the Council, the City Attorney and the Board of Zoning Adjustment for refusing to hear its application for a 220-foot-high-rise office building at 26th and Arapahoe. Also in September, the City Council gave preliminary approval to a 125-foot office building at the northeast corner of Ninth Street and Canyon Boulevard. Two months later, that request was defeated in a four to four tie vote. The final report of the Committee of architects studying height recommended that some buildings as tall as 300 feet should be allowed in certain areas of Boulder.

In June, it was rumored that the role of the Zoning Board might be headed for a major revision. City Councilman James Bowers, City Manager Ted Tedesco and City Planning Director Bill Lamont wanted the power of

granting zoning exceptions taken out of the hands of the Board and given to the Council. George Boland took the Council to task for its attempts to remove the Board's powers. City Attorney Walt Wagenhals interpreted the City Charter to say that it gave the Board the power to grant variances only, not exceptions, a role which had fallen to the Board by tradition and one which the Board had originally opposed.

A request to rezone the old Holiday Drive-In Theater property at Colorado Avenue and 30th Street received preliminary approval. The project was the largest single real estate development in Boulder at that date. Covering 13.8 acres, it was to provide 446 units in a central high-rise apartment building and four clusters of townhouses.

Timothy Fuller had proposed to establish a coffeehouse theater near the University Hill business district. Supporters contended that the establishment would help solve the "hippie problem". In July, the Zoning Adjustment Board turned down the request for the use variance in the multi-family residential zone.

#### DOWNTOWN

In January, City Attorney Walt Wagenhals was drafting a mall act for introduction to the Colorado General Assembly to allow a special assessment district method for financing a downtown mall.

The city's first purchase of land for Boulder Tomorrow was authorized unanimously by the City Council. The 10,000 square feet at the northwest corner of Broadway and Arapahoe was purchased for \$65,000 from Conoco which had a service station on the site. The money for the land came from funds which had been budgeted for expansion of the Municipal Building. Mayor Knecht pointed out that the property was critical in Boulder Tomorrow plans not only for the civic center complex, but for the widening of Arapahoe

Avenue.

In September, after two years of work, Victor Gruen Associates gave Boulder the final plans for the development of the downtown area. It had four key elements: The construction of off-street parking structures; a civic center; improved traffic circulation in the core area; and the construction of a superblock and pedestrian malls. Approval of the general concept of the Gruen plan was given by the City Council. The cost for three parking structures in the first phase was estimated at \$250,000. Downtown businesses were asked to form a general improvement district to finance them. City Manager Tedesco said that the civic center could be paid for with franchise fees from the Public Service Company if the proposed 20-year franchise were adopted. The streets could be financed with bonds floated from sales tax revenues under the Charter amendment approved at the November 4th election.

A bond issue was planned for 1970 to obtain voter approval for construction of the civic center. Winning architects in the Boulder Tomorrow civic center design competition were Hatami & Associates, Rogers Nagel and Langhart, and Easton Grabow & Associates.

1970

### PLANNING AND ZONING

The Boulder Valley Comprehensive Plan was characterized as the beginning, not the end of planning efforts. The key to its super-structure was the sub-community concept: Self-contained cities-within-a-city, each surrounded by parks, streams, and open areas; and each containing its own schools, churches, library, and shopping center.

In January, the City Attorney advised Council that they had no

jurisdiction over the proposed use of the Carmel House. The Colorado Department of Health had arranged for the building at 1005 12th Street to be used as an interim care facility for mentally retarded adults. Some residents of the area had petitioned the Council to prevent such use.

A high-rise-related lawsuit initiated in 1969 against the city by Western Industries was dismissed by the courts.

The City Council denied a planned south Boulder project. King Soopers grocery had requested approval for their locating on a site at Table Mesa Drive across from Stanford Avenue. Cited as reasons for the denial were the large building size, increased traffic flow, and its location across the street from a proposed subsidized housing project. In July, King Soopers filed a suit in District Court seeking to reverse the rezoning denial.

Site work began in the spring for the first homes in the Wonderland Hills subdivision. In the fall, a master plan proposing 603 more residential units was submitted to the planning office.

In May, according to a "Camera" article by Bill Hoffman, nearly the entire adult population of the Cordry subdivision turned out to protest a rezoning request for properties facing the frontage road along 28th Street. The Planning Board followed the staff's recommendation and voted six to one to deny the rezoning.

The Reverend James McKeown, Pastor of St. Johns Episcopal Church, had been seeking a way to open a hostel or campground with showers and toilets for summer transients in Boulder. He applied for a use variance from the Zoning Board and for a variance from the building codes from the Board of Building Appeals so he could use a site at Sixth Street and Canyon Boulevard. In May, his appeal to City Council to speed the process was denied. The Board of Zoning Adjustment granted a variance in June to permit, with conditions, overnight sleeping at the site. In July, City

Attorney Walt Wagenhals said he would file for revocation of the variance because certain of those conditions had not been met. According to an article in the "Camera" on July 10th, if sponsors of the "youth hostel" did not install permanent toilet and shower facilities, the zoning variance would be terminated.

In September, a site plan was approved for a 90-unit townhouse complex on 4.7 acres on the north side of Table Mesa Drive at Stanford.

#### SHOPPING CENTERS

In March, negotiations were under way for the purchase of land at 33rd Street and Arapahoe for the development of a regional shopping center.

#### DOWNTOWN

In April, Mayor Robert W. Knecht said the city might have to levy a one-mill property tax to finance the proposed \$7 million bond issue for the Boulder Tomorrow civic center. (The city had hoped to finance the center with revenues from a three per cent franchise fee which the Public Service Company would pay if the proposed franchise were accepted.) The bond issue would permit the expansion of the Municipal Building and the Public Library into larger complexes designed to fulfill many unmet municipal needs. New facilities to be constructed would include a new city hall. The old building would then be converted to a public safety center. Other construction would include a 500-seat theater, a conference and meeting hall, an exhibition hall, and additional parking spaces. A month before the June election, a financing plan was suggested that included the temporary imposition of an additional two-mill property tax levy.

### PLANNING AND ZONING

In February, City Planning Director Bill Lamont spoke in favor of a proposed sign code for the community.

... the comprehensive use of signs has ... become an "over-use". Signs have become major contributors to the visual pollution of our environment. The intent of the proposed sign code for Boulder is to permit the reasonable use of signs, with consideration given to both the right of the businessman to identify his business and the environmental rights of the public.

Last year's site of a transient hostel at Sixth and Canyon became a proposed site for a \$5.5 million high-rise hotel and convention center with adjacent luxury apartment houses. Architect James Hunter requested rezoning of the 10.5 acres bounded by Third and Eighth Streets, Canyon Boulevard, and Boulder Creek. The site had once before been planned as a hotel, but the developer had gone bankrupt, and since the early 1960's, his project, the Park Allen Hotel, had been known as "the ruins". The Council agreed to the site plan in September despite some concern over whether they had earlier approved the proposed heights of one 60-foot and one 70-foot apartment building along with the 114-foot hotel. The plan had been delayed because a Council meeting scheduled for August had been cancelled due to the lack of a quorum. The new height ordinance which would have subjected the buildings to a 35-foot limitation had taken effect September 3. The Council established that construction would have to begin within 24 months.

In July, after three years of debate, the Council approved a compromise height ordinance which limited buildings to 35 feet in residential, to 55 feet in commercial and multi-family residential areas, and to 140 feet after special review in the general areas of downtown and

the Crossroads Center. The ordinance also set building bulk, setback and open space requirements. PLAN-Boulder immediately countered the proposal with a petition drive to place on the November ballot a measure proposing a 55-foot height limit throughout the community.

In an article in the "Daily Camera" of March 12, Larry Knudsen reported that, "For nearly a decade the City Council has rejected plans for another supermarket in the Table Mesa area. (Now) Judge John B. Barnard (of the District Court has) ordered the council to grant a rezoning application that clears the way for the construction of three buildings - a (King Soopers) supermarket, an office building and a dental clinic." In April, after Council had adopted the court-ordered ordinance, it was announced that 13 residents of the Table Mesa area had hired a lawyer to appeal the case to the Colorado Supreme Court. The enactment of the rezoning ordinance was delayed until attorneys for King Soopers could file an answer to the citizen's action. In June, the appeal motion was denied. The residents then filed suit against the city and King Soopers protesting the rezoning approval because, they claimed, the Council had neither given notice of public hearings nor had they held any hearings before the ordinance was adopted. District Judge Howard O. Ashton dismissed the second suit.

In another "historic" rezoning matter, the Council gave preliminary approval in March for a planned development at Broadway and Juniper. This was John S. Hatch's fourth attempt to build on that site.

In July, the Council voted in favor of the first phase subdivision plat and final site plan of the 67-acre, 287-unit Tantra residential development south of Table Mesa Drive near the Turnpike.

Another favorable vote went to the annexation and rezoning of a 26-

acre tract for residential development north of Wonderland Hill Avenue. The acceptance came despite opposition of nearby residents who expressed concern for the natural setting and the wildlife in the area.

#### DOWNTOWN

At a Planning Board meeting in June, members discussed the one-way downtown loop and recommended that Council approve the preliminary plans for a Broadway-14th Street system. This would impact the area around Washington School, the old railroad depot at 14th Street and Canyon, part of Boulder High School's Recht Field track, and the Public Service Company's substation at the foot of the hill on Broadway. In July, petitions were circulating against the loop system.

In April, an ordinance establishing a Boulder Redevelopment Authority, required in order to implement the federally-sponsored Neighborhood Development Program (NDP), was scheduled for consideration. The initial NDP proposal was for the area bounded by 17th Street, Folsom, Athens, and the alley between Walnut and Pearl Streets. Property owners in the area were to be helped so their homes would meet housing code standards.

In September, the Planning Board denied a PUD and height exception for a office building across Boulder Creek from the Municipal Building, because of overintensive use of the land and parking problems. An application by the City of Boulder for rezoning of land on the south side of Boulder Creek, a PUD and height variance were approved to allow expansion of the Public Library.

Among other planning actions, the Council passed ordinances: rezoning

1974

### PLANNING AND ZONING

The Boulder County Consortium of Cities, an informal organization working to solve the county's land use problems, became an official body early in 1974.

This was the year of the Pow Wow rezoning battle. The president of Pow Wow Inc. said in May that the non-profit organization had again proposed a land trade that would provide a new location for the annual horse show and rodeo. The Pow Wow group had applied for rezoning of its property to enable it to sell the land to investors. The city Planning Director's reply was that both the zoning and the comprehensive plan maps showed the area as a public park. A long-time Boulder resident, Rollie Leonard, had given a 20-acre pasture at 28th and Spruce Streets to Pow Wow Inc. in 1947. A little more than 10 years later, the Association, suffering from taxes and other expenses, gave 11.5 of those acres to the city with the stipulation that Pow Wow have perpetual use of the area for its events, and that the land be used at other times for recreation. In the 1960's, when the YMCA was seeking a permanent location, the Pow Wow agreed to the proposal that the facility be located on that city land. In 1965, when the Junior Achievement group needed space, Pow Wow leased them land from its remaining 8.3 acres. When the JayCees needed to relocate the historical railroad depot, Pow Wow permitted them to locate the building on their land. In addition, three softball diamonds, a floodwater holding

pond, and the right-of-way for the eastern extension of Mapleton Avenue had whittled away at the parking space for Pow Wow events. The Association asked the city to provide them with 100 acres of land with improvements to replace their existing structures at the old site. The city was offered the 8.3 acres remaining as well as taking into consideration the 11.5 acres given them in 1958. This was not deemed feasible by the city. Through a rezoning request, Pow Wow then sought to increase the value of its land for resale so they could acquire a larger piece of property outside the city for their events. In November, the Council turned down the rezoning request, and in December, a lawsuit was filed against the city in response to that denial.

"Camera" reporter Barry Koltnow, in an article dated May 1, said that the Council had unanimously, if informally, agreed that the administration should make a detailed analysis of enhancing the core area in order to increase its density. Concurrently, suburbanization which had been creating a "sprawl" situation, should be halted.

The Planning Board in May denied the request for a two-year permit extension for a development (Sherrelwood) on land under consideration for the 28th-30th Streets one-way link.

At a meeting in June, two specific redevelopment goals of the city were agreed upon: That the Council is committed to the redevelopment of the downtown area; and that the redevelopment of the 28th Street shopping area is desirable.

In September, the Planning Board voted to recommend that the City Council approve rezoning from Industrial-Established to High Density Residential-Redeveloping the area along Pearl Street from 18th east to 21st Streets to insure separation of the downtown commercial district from its

28th Street counterpart.

In October, the City Council approved a major rezoning ordinance which opened the door for the development of more housing on Walnut and Pearl Streets and on some streets connecting them. Pearl Street property owners claimed their businesses would be adversely affected by the ordinance.

The Planning Board voted six to one to table consideration of a request that a residential development (Robinson) receive city approval to receive services from the Boulder Valley Water and Sanitation District. The approval would be deemed logical only if other services, including schools, transportation, police, fire, and parks and recreation facilities could be provided.

### SHOPPING CENTERS

Shopping center fever was high in 1974. In April the city made the decision to grant water and sewer services for a major expansion of the Gunbarrel shopping center. There had been persistent rumors that a shopping center developer was urging Niwot to incorporate so the town could be in control of its zoning. Boulder City Manager Archie Twitchell said that expansion of the existing center would forestall any new development. Tom Rogers of the Downtown Business Association said his group would prefer a community center at Gunbarrel, a major center in the Arapahoe-28th Street area, and one major department store downtown. He joined the City Manager and Councilman Frank Buchanan in their views that some kind of subsidization was going to be necessary to make the city's shopping center plans feasible.

The chairman and president of Western Industries announced in December that an agreement had been reached with Ralph Biernbaum, a Connecticut developer, to construct a major shopping center in the Arapahoe Village

locale.

Late in the year, incorporation for the Gunbarrel area was being proposed by the same developers who earlier had wanted Niwot to become a city. The County had given an unfavorable response to any change in zoning, so again a new municipal government was needed which could rezone without county intervention in the establishment of a shopping center.

#### DOWNTOWN

Early in 1974, after seven years of discussion and planning, the Council passed a resolution stating their intention to establish a downtown mall. City Manager Archie Twitchell stated that he recommended a city contribution of 50 per cent (\$650,000) of the estimated \$1.3 million cost for seven blocks of a pedestrian mall. The city's share would be from part of its allocation from the federal Better Communities Act. The Act was set up primarily for funding low- and moderate-income housing programs, so Boulder's mall plans would have to be tied to its housing program to qualify for the funds. Mr. Twitchell said that the unused second floors of downtown buildings might be converted to housing and that downtown rejuvenation would lower fire and police costs for residents.

In August, the City Council gave unanimous approval to a plan to close seven blocks in the downtown area to automobile traffic, creating three pedestrian malls. The vote, which came on a motion from Councilman Kenneth Wright, started a 180-day process during which a replacement for the streets would be designed. Owners opposing the mall concept represented 13.8 per cent of the property fronting the streets proposed for pedestrian areas. These were Pearl Street from 11th to 15th, 13th Street from Spruce to Walnut, and Walnut from 13th to Broadway. In December, members of the Council decided to "get by" with a four block surface mall

that was predicted to cost about \$1.8 million.

At mid-year, the Council was studying a proposed historic preservation code which would create a Historic Landmarks Board to recommend specific buildings and neighborhoods for preservation. The study was in response to the destruction of several older edifices.





## CHAPTER V

## ENVIRONMENT AND CULTURE

Some Boulder citizens of each generation were aware of the natural beauty around them and the need to preserve and enhance it. The federal government and philanthropic individuals had donated park lands to the community during the early years. Not until the environment began to be threatened did the city decide to try to protect it.

Other donations had been made to further educational, cultural and recreational opportunities in Boulder. Demands for more activities grew as the community grew.

1965

### PARKS AND RECREATION

In a "Daily Camera" article dated March 2, it was reported that the Boulder Parks and Recreation Advisory Board refused to recommend the leasing of a portion of North Boulder Park to the Community Hospital Association which wanted to develop some off-street parking on the land between the existing tennis courts and the hospital. Board members, however, did recommend another lease of park property to the YMCA. The land involved was the western 200 feet of Pow Wow Park along 28th Street which had been donated to the city by the Pow Wow Association.

Columbia Cemetery, located on 10 acres bordering Ninth Street near College Avenue, dates back to 1869 when the Masonic Lodge initiated action to buy the site. Later the Odd Fellows joined with the Masons to develop the cemetery, then conveyed the property to the Park Cemetery Association. When that Association became defunct in 1965, the burial grounds were

unkempt and had been the target of vandalism. In May, the city agreed to assume responsibility for the appearance of its grounds.

In this year, the Parks and Recreation Department established a Junior Ranger Force. A combination of work and recreation highlighted the summer for 42 teenage Boulder boys.

It was reported in August that Boulder Mountain Park lands now stretched continuously for five-and-a-half miles north to south just west of the city and for much of that distance abutted the city limits. Within that stretch lay 3,326 acres of city-owned parks. Another 131 acres lay in isolated mountain areas.

In an article in the "Camera" dated August 31, the Parks and Recreation Advisory Board discussed the Chautauqua Reservoir road. A request for access from that road across a strip of park land to property for which a county building permit had been acquired, was denied. The Board had a policy that generally opposed granting access to private property across park lands.

In October, Councilman Howard Klemme suggested that the city explore the possibility of working out a cooperative program with the county for acquiring land along Boulder Creek. He said that "open space" funds from the federal government might be available.

1966

### PARKS AND RECREATION

The "Camera" of February 8 reported that a Nine Point Program outlining plans for the development of Enchanted Mesa and nearby areas was referred to the City Council after lengthy consideration by the Parks and Recreation Advisory Board. Enchanted Mesa is the land south of the

Chautauqua grounds and east of the Flatirons that the city acquired from Kenneth Mirise after city voters had approved a bond issue for that purpose on July 10, 1962. The owner had proposed commercial development of the area including a "luxury" hotel.

The Advisory Board recommended that the area be kept in as natural a state as possible and that the Chautauqua Reservoir Road be designated as a family trail approach to Enchanted Mesa, but should be maintained only as an emergency road for rescue or fire control work and for reservoir service.

The majority of the Council favored opening the road to vehicles to make the Mesa more accessible to all citizens. Other plans suggested for the Enchanted Mesa included an Interpretive Center (Nature Museum) for the upper area of the Bluebell Shelterhouse road. This spot claimed the most variety of ecological habitats within a short distance.

Also in February, the Council rejected a conditional gift of \$23,000 for the purchase of 10 acres of park land at the mouth of Boulder Canyon. The donor, Thorne Ecological Foundation required that its funds be used to preserve a natural area. The city already owned eight acres in the vicinity including more than six acres around the Red Rocks which had been granted to the city in 1899 and two acres at Settlers' Park purchased in 1965 for \$18,000. In June, after the donated land had been appraised at \$29,000, the city agreed to pay the balance over the gift money. The owners of the property then increased their price to \$37,000. In July, the Council decided to condemn the land rather than pay that price or allow the property to be developed as an apartment house above the "Blue Line". In August the asking price was lowered to \$30,700, and the Council finalized the purchase.

A recommendation was made in July that North Boulder Park be renamed the H. Paul Crouch Park as a memorial to the former mayor who had died June 25.

As a part of the 1967 budget-making process, the Council knocked out the "Moon Mountain" playground that had been proposed for Scott Carpenter Park. The \$30,825 that would have been spent for the playground would be set aside for future park land acquisition. Opponents of the "moonscape" argued that park sites should be bought as soon as possible because of the continuing rise of land prices and the explosive growth of population in and around the city. One, Ruth Wright, submitted a petition with 600 signatures urging more emphasis be put on land acquisition.

A Council committee was meeting during 1966 with the Colorado Game, Fish and Parks Commission to resolve the question of state maintenance of Glacier Lake as a recreational area.

A proposal for a \$552,000 community center was presented to the Parks and Recreation Board. The request included offices, meeting rooms, a multi-purpose area and rifle range, arts and crafts facilities, game rooms, landscaping, and parking for 500 cars. Plans for an indoor swimming pool, handball courts and an ice-skating rink were included, but construction of those could be postponed. Land costs were not in the proposal but it was suggested that between 20 and 40 acres would be necessary for the center.

#### OPEN SPACE

On November 22, the "Daily Camera" printed a lengthy article with the heading "Open Space Concept Wins Full Support of Council". The proposal was made by the Parks and Recreation Board. It called for an aggressive effort to extend the city's mountain park system, to acquire or control land along the creek banks and arterial highways, and to separate Boulder

from the growing metropolitan area by a broad greenbelt around the perimeter of the city. John Adams, Chairman of the Parks Board, recommended land acquisition for the open space program be a major part of a \$1,850,000 general obligation bond. It was the consensus that spiraling land costs and accelerated development were forcing prompt action of the acquisition of open space. Oakleigh Thorne, a Parks Board member, pointed out that a similar plan, put together five years before, had been delayed by the water bond election, the sewer bond election, and the city sales tax election. "This time", he said, "it seems imperative that the proposal be put to the voters."

#### LIBRARIES

In October, the City Council gave tentative approval for a proposed branch library which would cost an estimated \$80,000 and would be financed partly by one bequest from the late George F. Reynolds, former English professor at the University, and another from a second estate. The total was \$36,500. Paul Friggens, Chairman of the Library Commission, said recommendations for a branch library had been two years in the works and had been studied and reaffirmed by the Planning Department.

1967

### PARKS AND RECREATION

The controversial "Moon Mountain" playground, cut during the budget sessions the previous fall, was authorized by the Council's six to two vote in January. The approval came after it was learned that the cost would be borne by the federal government, and the funds would be jeopardized and probably lost, if the project were not approved. Parks and Recreation Director Dwain Miller said the city had received \$66,000 in grants through

the Federal Land and Water Conservation Fund. The Council defeated a motion made by Councilman Charles Haertling in March to reconsider their January decision. In June, the first piece of equipment, a rocket climber, was purchased, and detailed plans and specifications had been prepared. Construction was to begin on receipt of the first check from the federal government.

In February, the donation of \$100,000 for a nature center, on condition that it be located south of Baseline Road and west of Bluebell Canyon, also caused controversy. The possibility of including Park and Recreation Department offices as well as a two-acre parking lot for 150 cars in an area of natural open space were prime factors in the opposition to acceptance of the gift. The city returned the money to the donors in March.

A contract was awarded in April for improvements at Harlow Platts Park. The dam was to be renovated and the lake reshaped. Funds for the project included money raised by a \$50 voluntary assessment against each lot in the Table Mesa subdivision. In November, the city earmarked \$36,000 for the purchase of an additional two acres at Harlow Platts Park. The land included 11 lots scheduled for development.

#### OPEN SPACE

City Manager Tedesco informed the Council in August that most of the acquisitions of "open space" had been in the mountains. When the mountain area of 3,000 acres and the municipal golf course and the airport were deducted, only 400 acres were left in the form of urban parks. "Present acquisition methods are not doing the job. A grand scheme is the only way to achieve the open space concept." The proposal called for an allocation of 40 per cent of funds collected through an addition of one per cent to

the sales tax to purchase a greenbelt around the city.

#### LIBRARIES

Federal matching funds of \$53,513 were approved for the proposed Table Mesa branch library. The grant raised the total budget for the facility to \$109,726.

In September, the Boulder Public Library joined a supplementary reference and inter-library loan service provided by the Denver Public Library.

Also in September, Miss Nell Buckingham bequeathed \$5,000 to the Library. Her other contribution to the city, before her death, was the furnishing of the Library's east reading room.

#### ENVIRONMENTAL PROTECTION

An new ordinance called for property owners to cut all weeds over 12 inches tall. If the weeds were not cropped within seven days after notification, the city sent someone to do it and charged the owner the cost of cutting plus 15 per cent.

1968

### PARKS AND RECREATION

In the continuing saga of "Moon Mountain", the project was rejected by the Council in March because construction bids were almost double the first estimate of two years before. Although federal funds would have paid half the costs, too many people objected to the "defacement" of trees and lawns, and to the allocation of so much money to one area of the city.

In April, Boulder's first Director of Parks and Recreation, W. Dwain Miller, resigned. He had held the position since 1961 and had been

instrumental in establishing the greenbelt program.

Mayor Robert W. Knecht appointed a committee of five Boulder men to study the use of sculpture in the city and study the design of its public facilities. Chairman was Carl Worthington, a member of the city Planning Board and one of the first proponents of the committee.

The Council approved an option to purchase two acres of land owned by the Boulder Valley School District at 21st Street and Canyon Boulevard. The option allowed the city to lease the property for one dollar a year for three years. If the city should acquire land particularly suited for a school site but valued at less than the Canyon property (\$80,000) the School District would accept the new site in payment for the higher priced Canyon Park land.

Paul Swoboda, Recreation Superintendent, recommended leasing the lower floor of the County Annex building at 14th and Spruce Streets for the senior citizen's programs which were then being scheduled at the Carnegie Library building on Pine Street.

#### OPEN SPACE

On February 28, a "Denver Post" article announced that "City Manager Ted Tedesco is negotiating with Boulder Memorial Hospital for purchase of 240 acres of greenbelt land." When this purchase was completed, the city would have nearly 1,000 acres of open space either purchased or leased for future purchase along the mountain backdrop.

In April, the Council named a Citizen's Advisory Committee on Open Space. Members were Robert W. McKelvey, Joyce Davies, and Edwin L. Wolff. James Bowers represented the Council. City Planner Bill Lamont, Director of Parks and Recreation Dwain Miller, Director of Public Works Andy Briscoe, Assistant City Manager Larry Blick, and County Planning Director

Robert Trenks, were staff representatives.

Joseph G. Wing was hired by the City Manager to be the first administrator of the program. Since his duties included right-of-way acquisition, his salary came from the general fund rather than from open space money. Guidelines for implementation stated that greenbelt funds should be spent only for land acquisition including the purchase of easements and options, for paying debt service, and for the protection of open space land from damages, destruction or impairment.

The Council authorized City Manager Tedesco to exercise the city's first purchase option on the Byron L. Wells property south of the National Center for Atmospheric Research. The option was the first of six the city held on the 645 acre tract. Each option, to be exercised annually, called for a \$138,000 expenditure.

#### LIBRARIES

Nearly \$4,000 was raised from a community auction held by the Boulder Rotary Club and all the proceeds were donated to the George F. Reynolds Branch Library. On October 14, despite a delay in the arrival of furniture, the branch was opened.

#### ENVIRONMENTAL PROTECTION

In a memorandum to the City Manager, an administrative assistant concluded that the unit cost of enforcement of a noise control ordinance was far out of proportion to the extent of the problem. Further, he felt that the effectiveness of measuring noise and enforcing an ordinance was next to impossible.

### PARKS AND RECREATION

Parks fees established by the Parks Board and approved by the Council were \$75 per dwelling unit for parkland acquisition in both single- and multi-family areas; \$50 per dwelling unit in multi-family areas for park improvements; and \$70 per dwelling unit in single-family areas for park improvements.

The H. Paul Crouch Memorial playground in North Boulder Park was dedicated in August with ceremonies which recalled the services of the late mayor.

In an article dated August 10, "Camera" reporter Bill Hoffman said that the Parks and Recreation Department had opened the Boulder Senior Citizen's Center at 14th and Spruce Streets in June using funds made available under the Older Americans Act. The first year, the city would provide 25 per cent of the costs in "in-kind services". The second year would cost the city 40 per cent and the third, 50 per cent. After that, the city would pay all the costs.

In December, the Parks and Recreation Advisory Board agreed with staff recommendations that no restroom facilities be constructed in Central Park, and approved the final site plans for Melody and Salberg Parks.

#### OPEN SPACE

The City Manager's Advisory Committee on Open Space recommended against elimination of the "Blue Line" until all mountain backdrop properties had been preserved.

In September, the Council authorized a contract for the purchase of

210 acres of greenbelt land immediately west of Boulder Memorial Hospital. Total cost of the project was \$244,000. Of that, the United States Department of Housing and Urban Development paid \$172,000 in grant money. The contract provided for three options, each for 70 acres, to be exercised in three years.

The city decided to appeal a District Court order that property taxes be paid on the Byron Wells land purchased through a series of options. The court interpretation was that the state statute exempting publicly-owned lands from taxation did not apply to lands held under option.

#### LIBRARIES

On March 2, the George F. Reynolds Branch Library was dedicated.

The "Wide Area Free Library Service" (WAFLS) moved from a trial period to permanent status. WAFLS was in inter-county cooperative system under which local public library cards were valid at all libraries throughout the Denver metropolitan area.

#### ENVIRONMENTAL PROTECTION

Some concerned citizens organized a group in early 1969 in an effort to do something about noise pollution. A Noise Control Committee was established at the request of the City Council and gathered data on the level of noise in selected locations and its effects on area residents. The Committee developed a program and recommended an ordinance to the Council in the fall. Action was delayed by opposition to the legislation by truckers who said it was too stringent, and by the Chairman of the Citizen's Committee who said it was too lenient.

The first documented case of Dutch Elm disease in the city was reported in the vicinity of 19th and Pine Streets. City Parks Superintendent Ron Donahue urged property owners within a seven-block radius to remove all

dead wood from their land. An ordinance was adopted in August which gave the city the authority to destroy or treat infected trees at the property owner's expense if private action had not been taken.

1970

### PARKS AND RECREATION

Boulder was among the top five cities in its class in 1970 from the standpoint of assessing and meeting community recreation needs. Parks and Recreation Director Robert Key listed 25 city park sites totalling 370 acres, and nine mountain and regional parks, including Boulder Reservoir, totalling 4,781 acres. Future plans envisioned two community centers for Boulder.

The Council made the decision in June to lease the Bergheim House at 1206 Euclid Avenue for use in the Parks and Recreation programs. The lease was for ten years with the option to purchase during that time.

In December the Boulder Reservoir was the topic of discussion at the Boulder Parks and Recreation Advisory Board meeting. The members discussed ways to finance a proposed \$1 million master plan which might include a golf course, amphitheatre and miniature railroad.

The Board granted a 15-foot easement to property near the Chautauqua Park Reservoir. In exchange, the city recieved four acres of greenbelt land near the mouth of Boulder Canyon.

#### OPEN SPACE

One of the goals for Boulder in 1970, suggested by Mayor Robert W. Knecht, was the development of bond issues to support long-range greenbelt programs.

In January, the Council authorized a nine-year pay-out agreement to purchase 284 acres of land southwest of the city for \$426,000.

Some residents in north Boulder organized a group called Friends of Wonderland Lake (FOWL) who circulated petitions requesting that the city buy much of the land surrounding the lake for greenbelt.

#### LIBRARIES

In a "Camera" article in December, Mary DeLapp said:

The Boulder Public Library under its forward-looking director, Marcelee Gralapp, now circulates not only books but staff members. Mrs. Marian Cooper and James Heckel give personal visitation service to homebound persons helping those with visual or physical handicaps to enjoy books. The library has some 200 titles of large-type books and some talking books.

#### ENVIRONMENTAL PROTECTION

A noise control ordinance was passed in January, and a noise control officer was hired.

1971

### PARKS AND RECREATION

In June, the Parks and Recreation Board heard from Bob Key, the Parks and Recreation Director, that the most pressing recreational need of the city was a community center. Envisioned were two such centers, one in the downtown area and one at Harlow Platts Park in south Boulder.

In August, the Board approved a revocable permit to the Boulder Valley School District for use of city park land as part of a parking lot for the new Fairview High School.

Board members passed a resolution instructing the staff to move ahead on a bond proposal for the November election. The issue would be for two

recreation centers and for additional tennis courts and ball fields. In November, controversy arose at a Board meeting over the proposed location of the North Boulder Park community center when it was charged that the plan would limit possible future expansion of Boulder Community Hospital. In December the Board voted to transfer a portion of park land to the hospital.

Beautifying Boulder with sculpture was one purpose of Aesthetic Action Inc., a private, non-profit group of citizens who prepared an aesthetic master plan for the city in 1971.

#### OPEN SPACE

Since January 1968, the city had purchased or optioned 2,223 acres of mountain backdrop and 50 acres on Davidson Mesa. To fulfill the greenbelt plan, 7,400 acres of open space still had to be preserved.

#### LIBRARIES

The Public Library began to "vastly expand" its services to the community. In addition to the normal circulation of books and magazines, the reference questions answered, the story hours and children's films, the Library started a Community Services Department. Free film programs in the auditorium, meeting rooms, outreach films, the municipal government reference center, a collection of films to check out, and a choice of records or cassette tapes, were available to the public.

#### ENVIRONMENTAL PROTECTION

During the first year under the noise control ordinance, 35 cases went to court. Ten were dismissed and 25 received convictions.

### PARKS AND RECREATION

On April 4, the "Camera"'s Bill Hoffman reported that in separate recommendations, the Planning and the Parks Boards at a special joint meeting both picked the southeast corner of Sixth Street and Canyon Boulevard as the preferred site for the North Boulder Recreation Center approved by voters last fall for Ninth Street and Balsam Avenue. The Planning Board motion specified that the city should trade all or part of its land between Community Hospital and Ninth Street for an equivalent amount of park land elsewhere which would be purchased by the hospital. The president of the Community Hospital Board of Directors said their "long-standing position" had been to purchase the adjacent city property for expansion. The 55-foot building height limit approved by voters in 1971 put "greater emphasis" on the hospital's earlier plans to acquire the land since they had planned to eventually construct a nine-story tower.

At a joint meeting of the Parks and Recreation and the Planning Boards on April 20, both Boards recommended that the City Council accept the offer by Community Hospital that they trade five acres of Long's Gardens, which the hospital had recently acquired, for the 2.7 acres of park property on Balsam Avenue at Ninth Street, and that the North Boulder Recreation Center be located on the Long's Gardens site.

The day after new city-installed lights were turned on for a night ballgame at Casey Junior High School, a petition signed by 41 persons living in the neighborhood was presented to City Council. They protested the use of the lights and the public address system. The Parks and Recreation Board agreed in November to move the Casey ball field light

towers to the Pow Wow grounds, as ordered by City Council.

The City Council unanimously overrode objections of Parks and Recreation administrators and opened the city's summer Ranger program to girls as well as boys.

#### OPEN SPACE

In granting approval to the Planned Residential Development at Wonderland Hill, the city had agreed to purchase 38 acres as greenbelt. In addition, the developers had agreed to give the city, within three years, the 23-acre Wonderland Lake after upgrading its dam. In December, 61 acres of land nearly surrounding Wonderland Lake became part of the city's open spaces.

The Council approved purchase of development rights only of 25 acres on Davidson Mesa.

In November, the Greenbelt Advisory Committee indicated they would support up to 25 per cent city financing to preserve the White Rocks natural area. Total financing could not be justified because the area would not be open to the public.

In a "Denver Post" article in November, Jane Cracraft reported that the City Council had unanimously approved a \$2 million greenbelt purchase. "The 1200 acres is in a 3.5 mile-long strip south of Boulder near Eldorado Springs and Marshall Lake. In 1964 the owners tried to develop the land, but the voters in a special election refused to extend utility service to the area." Open Space Land Program grants of \$172,000 in 1969, \$564,000 in 1971, and \$356,000 in 1972 were matched with city funds and used either for purchase of for early exercise of options.

#### LIBRARIES

The City Council took its first look at a proposed Boulder cable television (CATV) franchising ordinance in March. As proposed by Administrative Assistant Robert Sample, the measure would repeal a current ordinance under which Community Communications Company improved reception for customers in the University Hill area. The new ordinance would open the door for private firms to provide multi-channel CATV, a channel for the School District, the city government, and a public access channel. In June, Boulder advertised that the city was accepting proposals. Two firms, TelePrompter and Telecommunications, Inc. announced their intentions to submit proposals.

Directed by Marcelee Gralapp, the Public Library was already collecting video-tapes about local people, places and activities, getting ready for the advent of CATV.

Ground was broken in April for the new addition to the Library which would contain 15,000 square feet in two stories. One of these was the children's library and the second floor contained the non-print media center. Value of the completed project was estimated at \$795,000. some \$555,000 of which was approved in a bond election, \$165,000 from revenue-sharing money, and \$75,000 from a federal grant.

1973

### PARKS AND RECREATION

In February, the Parks and Recreation Advisory Board reviewed a proposed sculpture to be placed at the intersection of Broadway and Baseline Road. The artist for the project was E. K. Field, and the

estimated cost was between \$8,000 and \$10,000. It was to be the first major project to be implemented in the Aesthetic Master Plan. In August, the Council voted to contribute \$5,000 towards the project.

The Board authorized the purchase from the School District of 2.14 acres at Canyon Boulevard and 22nd Street for a city park. In December, they turned down an offer to obtain the Pow Wow grounds in a \$450,000 land exchange. They requested that negotiations be continued in an attempt to purchase the Lake Valley Golf Course.

#### OPEN SPACE

The Director of the Parks and Recreation Department, Leonard Ehrler, and Superintendent of Parks, Ronald Donahue stated that 25 per cent of park staff time was now spent on the maintenance of greenbelt areas and they requested "emergency" funds from the open space budget. Since the program began, 43,000 acres had been acquired. One recent acquisition had been the Hidden Valley Ranch which would require maintenance. The city was negotiating for the Sawhill Pond area which would need restoration and maintenance. Acting City Manager Bill Lamont expressed the opinion that a public body should take charge of the program and that the Parks and Recreation Department would have to be responsible for upkeep. Councilman Kenneth Wright objected to the proposed change because he felt that the parks people were too oriented towards improvement and planting, not in retaining natural areas.

In June, Acting Manager Lamont announced a reorganization of the greenbelt program. This included keeping the Advisory Committee on Open Space and expanding it from four to five members. He said both the Real Estate Director and the Director of Parks, Recreation and Open Space would serve as staff and resource people for the Citizen's Committee.

In August the Council debated the merits of forming a separate city department for the open space program. They later accepted an alternative proposal from City Manager Archie Twitchell. That plan created the position of Open Space Coordinator and provided for the creation of an Open Space Board. In December, the Council appointed five persons to a newly-created Open Space Board of Trustees who would act as an advisory body to Council on goals, priorities, budget, and the uses and maintenance of open space land.

#### LIBRARIES

In March, the City council approved by a six-to-two vote a contract for cable television services with TelePrompTer. Some of the aspects of the contract were: a return of \$2.4 million over a 10-year period into local programs; complete undergrounding of cables within the 15-year contract period; and contract renegotiation every three years. The following November, the "Wall Street Journal" reported that TelePrompTer was in financial difficulties.

1974

### PARKS AND RECREATION

The South Boulder Recreation Center was opened to the public on January 2.

Parks and Recreation Director Leonard Ehrler told the Council that Boulder's Junior Ranger program was not exempt from the increased minimum wage law. The cost of the program which employed about 115 young people would increase four-fold under the new law.

Two Boulder men banded together to form a group called the Committee to Recall the Sculpture. They stated that their primary goal was to keep

future sculptures from being erected without public comment.

In July, a grass-roots movement to erect a community cultural center was under way. The idea was a project of the Boulder Council on the Arts and Humanities.

The Senior Citizen's Center was outgrowing its facilities at the former Elks Lodge, and the city expressed its intention to build a new center.

In the spring, the Parks Board endorsed the concept presented by the Flatirons Companies to take gravel from a 445-acre tract stretching from the Boulder-Denver Turnpike to a point near Marshall Road, then reclaim the land and give it or sell it at a minimal price to the city for a regional park. The land in question had been optioned by the Flatirons Companies.

#### OPEN SPACE

The Open Space Board of Trustees, established by Ordinance #3940 in September 1973, formulated a plan and a list of priorities for acquisition. During the first months of meetings, the new Board complained that reports and studies had not been written because of lack of staff. Environmentalists complained that the Open Space Coordinator had not been given the staff or the power to operate effectively and that land had not been acquired with available funds. The Board was urged to use the \$4 million bonding capacity before inflation diminished its value. In May, the Board suggested that the administration should reorganize the greenbelt program in a way which would eliminate the position of Coordinator.

By June, the Open Space Board of Trustees, having wrestled with the Flatirons Companies issue for three months, presented a compromise to Council. That proposal, according to "Camera" reporter Barry Koltnow, would have allowed the Companies to gravel about 315 acres over a 15 to 30 year period during which time the Companies would reclaim the land and

return it as a gift to the city for use as a regional park. The remaining land around the Creek and the Turnpike would remain in a natural state. The concept of returning the land to the city hinged on a favorable ruling by the Internal Revenue Service which would have provided Flatirons with a tax benefit.

In other greenbelt matters, the City Council approved the acquisition of the \$625,000 MacKenzie Farm south of Jay Road and east of the Longmont Diagonal. In July they authorized the purchase of 70 acres adjacent to Boulder Reservoir at a cost of \$3,150 per acre with an option to purchase an additional 47 acres. With the purchase in December of 907 acres, the open space acquisitions for 1974 totalled 1,397 acres for \$3.07 million.

The Council unanimously approved the Boulder Valley Open Space plan in October after one addition made by Land Officer Don Walker and two amendments. The addition was to change the priority of acquiring lands along Boulder Creek from third to first. The County Board of Health decision to allow construction of a septic tank at Third Street and Canyon Boulevard precipitated the request for change. In an amendment, the Council clarified the fact that open space funds may only be used for those activities allowed in the ordinance that established the purpose of open space.

In October the Board of Trustees proposed purchase of two first-priority areas; the 156-acre McCann property at the south end of the Mesa Trail and the 320 acres of land in the Mount Sanitas area owned by Wittemeyer interests.

In November, the Board voted to recommend to Council three purchases; 78 acres near the White Rocks area, three acres north of Eldorado Springs along the base of the mountains, and 68 acres north of Baseline Road and

west of South Boulder Creek known as the Burke II property.

#### LIBRARIES

The new children's room at Boulder Public Library opened in July. Improvements still to come in the upstairs portion of the new addition were to include rooms for public meetings and a television studio.

The Boulder Public Library Foundation, a non-profit corporation, was established in 1974 to "help you help your library through tax-exempt gifts."

In January, City Manager Archie Twitchell announced that negotiations would begin immediately with a Boulder-based corporation to provide a city-wide cable television system. Samuel S. Street had obtained written agreements from both TelePrompter and Telecommunications Inc. to purchase their cable permits. Two months later the Boulder Cable Communications Study Committee recommended that the city revoke the TelePrompter permit instead of transferring it to Mr. Street, and put the cable franchise out for bids. In April, criticism of how Mr. Street received exclusive rights to negotiate the cable franchise began to cloud the issue, and he withdrew from the negotiations and insisted the city put the permit out to bid. He then submitted a \$17,000 bill to the city for his consulting work on the revised CATV permit.

Three companies bid for the system. S. S. Street and Associates, Ken Downes Associates, and Communication Corporation of Boulder. While the latter two agreed to accept the system on a permit basis, Mr. Street wanted a franchise granted by a vote of the people. In September, the Council chose Ken Downes Associates by a five-to-four margin over S. S. Street, and began formal negotiations for a city-wide system. Mr. Downes was given until January 1975 to line up the financing necessary to build the system.





## CHAPTER VI

## HOUSING AND HUMAN RELATIONS

Coping with the agenda items discussed in previous chapters left the city with little time or money to solve its other problems. One of the more identifiable was the lack of housing for the low- and moderate-income sector. Major controversies were to come from attempts to resolve the personal and emotional relations among an increasingly diverse community.

1965

### HOUSING

In August a county-wide meeting was held to discuss procedures for obtaining government funds to help house the elderly. At a meeting in December, a petition was filed requesting that the City Council explore the need to create a Housing Authority for Boulder.

### HUMAN RELATIONS

Discrimination in housing and employment were found to exist in Boulder by a temporary committee appointed by Mayor John Holloway in 1964. As a direct result, a Human Relations Commission was established in January by Ordinance No. 2862.

1966

### HOUSING

In January, the Council directed the administration to conduct a survey that would furnish information on the need for senior citizen

housing in Boulder. In June, they voted to establish a local Housing Authority by resolution, and in August, Mayor Robert W. Knecht named a five-member largely autonomous body empowered to determine the need for public housing, to plan and administer construction, sell the necessary bonds, establish admission criteria and rents, and manage the program.

### HUMAN RELATIONS

By March, Boulder's Human Relations Commission, chaired by Superintendent of Boulder Valley Schools E. G. Burnkrandt, had adopted a structure of three committees to serve in liaison capacities with various interested persons and groups. A manual of goals and procedures was approved in May. The Fair Housing Listing Service, a project adopted by the Commission, was set up to assist minority group members to find housing.

1967

### HOUSING

The United States Department of Housing and Urban Development (HUD) approved a "Workable Plan" for Boulder's improvement making the city eligible for federal aid for low-income elderly housing.

### HUMAN RELATIONS

The Chamber of Commerce decided in July to continue to sponsor the annual Easter cross and Christmas star on Flagstaff Mountain until it was informed that it could not display those symbols there. The cross and the star had been displayed annually for some 20 years. Inquiries had been received by the Human Relations Commission from some city residents who were concerned about religious symbols on public parkland.

In September, City Manager Tedesco created a new position of Administrative Assistant for Human Relations which would provide the Human Relations Commission with basic staff support.

1968

### HOUSING

A significant piece of legislation was the Housing Code, approved in May. Boulder would be the fourth city in Colorado to enact minimum housing standards for its residents. As a building code set structural standards, so a housing code legislated safety, health and aesthetic standards. Passage of the ordinance opened the way for federally-funded housing programs and urban renewal. To become certified to receive these funds, each city had to have a "Workable Program" which was an outline of procedures for total community improvement and which provided a system of checks and balances so the federal government could determine where its money was being spent. Boulder had lost an earlier certification because it had not had a housing code in effect for the required six months.

A survey showed a desperate need for at least 300 units of low-income housing in Boulder. The Boulder Housing Authority proceeded with a leasing application, one that supplemented the rental payments which low-income and elderly people were able to pay, because that program was not contingent on federal certification.

In December, Planning Director Bill Lamont suggested that 40 units proposed for the old City Yards site at Third and Pearl Streets be constructed partially by the Housing Authority and partially by the Turnkey Leasing Program, under which a private developer would construct the units then turn them over to the Authority.

### HUMAN RELATIONS

In April, the Council pledged its support and, when possible, its participation in the efforts of a committee of Boulder citizens to reduce racial problems and tension in the city. Charles Nilon, Professor of English at the University, said the group "feels that matters of race in our city are less gracious than they could be."

In June, about 20 "hippies" proposed to Boulder police that juvenile officers join with them to establish a half-way house for teen-age runaways.

In another meeting in June, a group led by representatives of the American Civil Liberties Union charged the Boulder Police Department and the Boulder County Sheriff's Office with "hippie" harassment and abuse. In July, Boulder authorities were cited for harassment in a challenge to the state vagrancy law. Police had arrested two men as vagrants in a "hippie flop house".

1969

### HOUSING

Housing Director John Hooyer announced in March that a check had arrived from the Department of Housing and Urban Development (HUD) that would allow the city's low-cost housing leasing program to get under way. By November, four sites for low-income housing had been approved by HUD. Three were for general occupancy at Baseline Road and Manhattan Drive, Colorado Avenue and 35th Street, and in the vicinity of Kalmia Avenue and 28th Street. A project for 50 units for the elderly at 1135 Portland Place was the fourth site approved.

In May, the Zoning Board had voted four to one to deny a rent-

supplement housing project planned by the Catholic Archdiocese of Denver at Moorhead and Ash Avenues. The Planning Board had voted in favor of the proposal.

### HUMAN RELATIONS

In response to an incident in December 1968, when two University of Colorado students had lighted a peace symbol on Flagstaff Mountain, the Council voted in February to ban all symbols, religious or otherwise, from city land. The Human Relations Commission which had taken a stand opposing display of a lighted cross during Easter 1969, broadened its position to include the Christmas star as well. No formal ordinance was introduced.

Other problems took precedence over symbols during the summer. In July, the Council passed three new ordinances aimed at alleviating the problems on University Hill. Two of these gave the police a stronger hand in controlling the loitering and the panhandling which had been a major concern of the Hill merchants. The third ordinance prohibited anyone from "living or sleeping or residing" in a parked vehicle on a public street. Hundreds of "hippies" attended the second Council meeting in July to protest the new laws. One speaker proposed that Central Park and the site of the Park Allen Hotel construction ruins at Sixth Street and Canyon Boulevard be turned into campgrounds for the transients.

The ordinance prohibiting sleeping in a vehicle was repealed in September.

In August, the Boulder City-County Health Department declared Central Park to be a public hazard. It had been used by 200 to 500 persons congregating for several hours each day without toilet or lavatory facilities. Health officials recommended that the city immediately close the park and enforce more stringently Boulder's Housing Code. In response, City Manager Ted Tedesco assigned a police officer and a sanitarian to

patrol the park, and prohibited concerts so there would be less incentive for large gatherings.

A special United Black Action Committee (UBAC) had been protesting membership policies of the Elks Club. They notified Mayor Robert Knecht that five Boulder blacks had not been allowed to join the local organization. In October, UBAC asked the city to review its contracts that furnished water and sewer services outside the city with an eye to adding non-discrimination clauses. The city had a revocable contract for these services with the Elks Club.

In November, the Human Relations Commission sponsored a program "Conflict or Community" aimed at focusing on solutions to the "hippie" problem.

1970

### HOUSING

A proposal to build a 24-unit low- and moderate-income housing development in the Table Mesa area on Gillaspie Drive ran into opposition from residents who wanted to retain single-family zoning.

The Housing Authority approved a proposal by the Denver Catholic Archdiocese to construct 36 housing units on the site of the former City Yards at Third and Pearl Streets.

In April, a contract was awarded for 96 units of low-cost housing for the University of Colorado.

St. Johns Housing Corporation built 150 apartment units for low-income families and individuals on 34th Street north of Valmont Road.

The Housing Authority received a \$1.6 million loan to construct a 95-unit apartment building at 1938 Walnut Street designed for elderly

residents.

#### HUMAN RELATIONS

Under an unofficial policy on contracts approved by the Council in February, non-discrimination clauses were to be included in all future revocable water and sewer permits, public works contracts, applications for licenses or licensing activities and renewals of such contracts. The inclusion was recommended by the Human Relations Commission. In a "Camera" editorial dated February 8, the opinion was expressed that "utilities are not a governing instrument but an impartial service. Because city utilities represent a monopoly service, their distribution should be kept on a service-for-payment basis, and problems of social justice should be left to the courts." At the second Council meeting in February, the Council approved an ordinance that included a non-discrimination clause in all future public works contracts with the city, but postponed a decision on similar language in utility agreements.

A policy prohibiting displays on the mountain backdrop west of the city was adopted in a five to four vote in February. Petitions were circulated asking the Council to reconsider that move. In March, the Council voted to allow the Christmas star to be lighted for that holiday while deciding against the Easter cross since it constituted a religious symbol. Council said they believed that freedom of speech did not extend to the guarantee of a captive audience.

Added to the thorny decisions on discrimination and "free speech" was the "hippie" problem which some citizens felt the Council should solve in 1970. In February, the Reverend James McKeown, Pastor of St. John's Episcopal Church, requested that some Council members meet with him to formulate plans for a youth hostel in Boulder. In May, Rev. McKeown

requested, directly from Council, variances from the zoning and the building codes for a transient campground on the old Park Allen Hotel site at Sixth Street and Canyon Boulevard. The request was denied on a motion that the Council did not have the power to grant the variances or the power to order the Boards of Zoning Adjustment and Building Appeals to grant the requests. Boulder's Human Relations Commission supported the efforts to provide the sleeping and sanitary facilities for transients.

In a letter sent to city residents in May, the City Council stated its policies relating to the expected behavior of both residents and visitors, and included synopses of pertinent city ordinances applicable to preserving the health, safety and harmony of the community. An ordinance was adopted that required permits for concerts in city parks.

The Boulder Urban Corps had been developed in January. In May, the University of Colorado Office of Financial Aid began referring qualified work-study students to the Boulder Urban Corps. The Ford Foundation funded the program to involve college students in meaningful urban activities.

In June, the Council pledged its support and City Manager Tedesco pledged law enforcement action to protect University Hill shoppers and merchants who said they were being harassed by "hippies". Some merchants later supported, then suspended, a recall move against the Council because of what they felt was a lack of action to improve conditions and a multiplicity of light sentences given to "hippies" who were arrested.

Boulder's Youth Service Bureau conducted a survey that indicated there were critical problems among young people in areas of the educational process, activities and places to go, adult relationships with youth, and drugs. A youth community center was debated during budget sessions in October, but was turned down by the Council in November.

Dave Whitlow, Administrative Assistant to the City Manager, advocated

in a report on the drug problem in Boulder that a relationship of increased trust be developed between law enforcement officers and the transient community. Efforts toward communication had been inaugurated by the first full-time Human Relations Officer hired by the city in November. Also recommended were support for methadone treatment of heroin addicts by the People's Clinic and support for the Community Free School which offered alternatives to those who did not choose a more formal structured education.

1971

### HOUSING

In February, Colorado's Court of Appeals upheld a Boulder District Court decision authorizing rezoning of a tract on Gillaspie Drive for low-cost housing. In May, the Colorado Supreme Court cleared the way for construction of the 24 units, but the project faced further delays because of federal financing problems.

The Housing Authority formally accepted and paid for the first 44 of a total of 292 new housing units being constructed under the federal "Turnkey" program. In September, they purchased 14 single-family homes at 1700 Iris Avenue. Construction of low-income apartments was halted at the Third and Pearl Streets site by an order issued by the Boulder City-County Health Department after tests confirmed high radiation levels at three spots on the land which had once housed a mill.

In a "Camera" article dated April 17, Ron Tollefson reported that a program of deferring low-income homeowner payments for street and sidewalk improvement projects was given the go-ahead by City Council. Described by Administrative Assistant Dave Whitlow as the first known program of its kind

in an American city, it could benefit at least 15 per cent of Boulder's citizens. City Manager Tedesco pointed out that much of the city's street and sidewalk construction work was centered in the older sections of Boulder where a significant number of low-income homeowners lived. He proposed the deferral program to ease their financial burden, as well as lessen resident opposition to the city projects based on foot assessment costs.

The Council adopted a law in August that would allow refunds of city property taxes paid by low-income elderly residents of the city.

#### HUMAN RELATIONS

In February, the Council attempted to fill in the generation gap when it appointed two high school youths as ex-officio members of the Human Relations Commission.

In April, the Human Relations Commission recommended that the city pass its own anti-discrimination policy, outlawing discrimination in employment, housing and public accommodations, and that it should create a Human Rights Department. In May, the Council directed the administration to begin drafting a local ordinance on human rights and discrimination.

The city was urged to implement an anti-drug-abuse program through information and education and treatment through a methadone clinic, to increase manpower in the Police Department, and possibly sponsor a youth coffeehouse through the Youth Service Bureau.

After a weekend of confrontation on the Hill between the "street-people" and the "establishment", there were extreme demands from both sides. A businessmen's committee stated it had "no confidence" in the way the city and Council had dealt with the transients, and called for replacement of Municipal Judge Richard Hansen because he had been too lenient with the

street people who had come into his court. The transients met and issued demands that the new Hill substation of the Police Department be removed and that the space be turned over to them, rent-free.

In October, after four months of interviews and research into court records, the JayCees released their findings on the causes and results of the University Hill riots. The report stated that Boulder had gone through several stages of transients. The first group were called "flower children" and there was never much official reaction to them. In the late 1960's, a group arrived who were apparently dedicated to the destruction of the establishment. They were seeking confrontation. The city chose to control illegal activities, but was not so oppressive as to give this group reason for erupting. Because of this, the city and courts earned a reputation for being soft on hippies. The third group appearing in 1969 and 1970 were mostly un-educated, non-working transients. As a result of drug use and related illegal activities, the police stepped up law enforcement and arrests. Increased tensions and the riots were the result.

1972

### HOUSING

The City Council reviewed an application for a subdivision plan in Martin Acres to construct 28 homes to rent to low- and moderate-income families. The seven-and-half acre site at Ash and Moorhead was the same location where a rezoning proposal by the Archdiocese of Denver to construct apartment units had been rejected based on the argument that the project would change the single-family character of the neighborhood. St. Thomas Aquinas Housing Corporation was the new applicant.

The Boulder Housing Authority had completed construction on 292 new

housing units for elderly persons and families at six locations by the end of July. It also leased 40 units at scattered locations throughout the city, and still had a waiting list of 200.

In August the proposed interim growth policy was presented to the Planning Board and quickly encountered a snag. Although Boulder residents had voted the previous November to hold the city's growth rate below the level experienced in the 1960's, they also expressed their wish to continue to provide low- and moderate-income housing for the community. Striking a balance between the two directives presented problems. Boulder builders could not construct the less expensive units without putting up several times as many higher income units in order to make a profit on their project as a whole, according to an article by Phil Gruis in the "Camera" dated August 18.

### HUMAN RELATIONS

In the continuing discrimination case against the Elks Club, Councilman Penfield Tate was representing the Boulder United Black Action Committee (UBAC). The two-year-old lawsuit, involving a question of city water and sewer services to the Club which limited its membership to white males, was before the District Court. In May, the Council supported Councilman Tate's role in the case just after adopting a conflict-of-interest ordinance. Mr. Tate withdrew as the UBAC attorney in August on advice from the Colorado Bar Association.

Despite a policy statement adopted by the Chamber of Commerce and read to the Council requesting that the city rely on federal and state anti-discrimination laws and "abandon further consideration" of the proposed Human Rights Ordinance, the measure was approved in July.

"Camera" staff member Marc Wilson reported on June 21 that Councilman

John Buechner had presented a policy statement on the summer transient problem on behalf of the entire City Council. In response, City Manager Tedesco requested an additional \$15,000 for law enforcement, appointment of a third municipal judge, and advised Council they could expect to pay more jail fees to the county. The Buechner policy statement also requested the "strengthening and improving (of) working relationships with civic, religious, and other organizations who are concerned with the transient." Councilman Tim Fuller responded to this section by requesting a \$9,000 allocation to the Council of Churches. The money would be used to hire five social workers to attempt to organize a self-help program in the Hill area.

In July, Ron Tollefson reported in the "Camera" that Les Olson was the Director of the new Department of Human Resources. "The department will serve as the research arm of the City's Human Resources Commission. It will also oversee work of the Youth Service Bureau, the Urban Corps, and the teen-center, Our House, and will represent the city in work with the runaway center, Haven House, the Boulder Communications Center, the Summer-Hill '72 task force, and with the local anti-drug abuse program."

In an article dated September 11, Tollefson reported in the "Camera" on the Summer-Hill '72 Task Force report:

Boulder's "street life" scene is a complex culture. ... (There are) the transients who are "on a road to maturity" and the street people who are "on a path to self-destruction". ... (During the summer of 1971), the use, transfer and sale of illegal drugs (became) a major aspect of the Hill scene. ... Since the community does not provide facilities such as overnight housing or rest rooms, the transients "are forced to seek out street people" for such facilities. ... The Summer-Hill '72 task force program included ... legal defender referrals, daily meals at Eben Fine Park, counseling, provision for rest-room facilities, and storage for back packs.

1973

### HOUSING

In January, a proposed amendment to the Housing Code requiring inspection and certification of all rental, motel, hotel and roominghouse units was reviewed by the Council. The first Housing Code was passed in 1968. There were approximately 10,000 rental units in the city in 1973, and since inspections began in 1970, some 4,600 had been completed. The rental housing licensing ordinance would provide for certification of all rental units on an annual basis and charge the landlords a fee for the inspection. Criticism came from members of the Rental Property Owners Association, and the Boulder County Tenant's organization supported the measure during a public hearing on February 20. Another hearing was held on March 20 and it, too, had to be continued because of the controversy aroused. The Council passed a rental housing licensing ordinance at their first meeting in April. Exemptions included in the measure were those houses rented out during an owner's temporary absence and those owner-occupied dwellings of no more than two units.

Councilman Tim Fuller introduced a rent control ordinance in October which would have included a roll-back of rents to January and permission for landlords to raise rents in order to earn a "fair and equitable" profit. In addition, landlords would be permitted to raise rents by two-and-one-half per cent, if their buildings passed housing code inspections without any violation. The proposal was not accepted by City Council.

### HUMAN RELATIONS

The city provided \$73,000 in matching funds towards a federal grant awarded in July to Boulder County. These helped pay for three new or expanded programs dealing with drug abuse: A drug rehabilitation program for transients in Boulder, expansion of the methadone clinic with more counseling for all abusers, and continuation of the work of Our House, a center for youth under 18.

According to an article by Barry Koltnow in the July 30 edition of the "Camera":

Human Resources Director Les Olson ... resigned his post this morning. ... (In April, five members of the Human Relations Commission had approached the Director of the Human Resources Department and demanded his resignation.) ... "The group said (that the Director and) the head of the Office of Human Rights had been 'dragging their feet' in the prosecution of discrimination cases. ... (There was a countercharge) that the commission had become ineffective due to the polarizing efforts of two of its members and had not been able to accept its loss of power that occurred when the department was formed." ... (On April 11, the Chairwoman and the Vice-Chairman of the Commission resigned in protest of the "vendetta" against the Director.) ... City Council temporarily suspended the commission's activities and then, on April 28, conducted a public hearing (held in private) on the issue ... . (The Council then) abolished the present commission and repealed the 1964 ordinance which created it. ... (An) 11-member Human Relations Study committee ... created a new Human Relations Commission ordinance ... (and is) involved in coming up with a second ordinance creating an official Human Resources Department. ... Olson's department is not official in that it was not created by ordinance. Former City Manager Ted Tedesco created it by letter in June 1972 ... .

The Council appointed a new Commission in October.

On December 18, the Council passed on first reading an amendment to the Human Rights Ordinance which would make it unlawful to discriminate in the area of employment on the basis of a person's age, marital status or sexual preference.

1974

### HUMAN RELATIONS

The sexual preference amendment, scheduled for second reading on New Year's Day, was postponed until February so that the Human Relations Commission could study it and file a report. The Commission sent a favorable recommendation to the Council. It included the suggestion that any expansion of the sexual preference legislation into areas such as housing and public accommodations be held off. Mayor Penfield Tate, who had sponsored the proposed ordinance, agreed.

In a "Camera" story dated March 1, Barry Koltnow reported that Mayor Tate "will withdraw the sexual preference amendment from council consideration and put it directly to a vote of the people. (He) said he will request a referendum vote ... within 90 days." In an article on March 6, Mr. Koltnow wrote, "Boulder City Council narrowly approved the 'sexual orientation' ordinance ... setting up a potential showdown with a group threatening a council recall. Council rejected a motion ... to send the issue to a vote of the people."

The Council also approved a second ordinance prohibiting job discrimination on the basis of marital status or age (40- to 65-years old) and setting up appeals procedures for discrimination complaints. The original discrimination ordinance had included all these clauses. The Council severed the parts and renamed the "sexual preference" measure to the "sexual orientation" ordinance.

The day following this Council vote, the Boulder Recall Committee announced that it would set in motion the procedures necessary to have a

recall election of the five Council members who voted in favor of the sexual orientation law.

On March 19, the Council agreed to put the law to a vote on May 7. They approved it on second reading, then delayed enactment until after the election. The ordinance was defeated at the polls in May by a nearly two to one margin.

More than 9,500 registered voters signed petitions for recall of Mayor Penfield Tate and Councilman Tim Fuller. In May, three persons filed a class action suit in District Court requesting the recall election be postponed until fall. The grounds for postponement were primarily the disenfranchisement of University students if the election were held in June. Judge William Neighbors issued a temporary restraining order blocking the Council from setting June 11 as the recall election date. Mayor Tate and Councilman Fuller received approval from the Council to proceed with a lawsuit against the city over an interpretation of the Charter. The City Attorney had ruled that the Councilmen could not be listed on the ballot as their own possible successors should they be recalled. City Attorney Walt Wagenhals filed a motion in District Court requesting the restraining order on the June election date be lifted. He also asked for a ruling on the issue of the two councilmen being allowed to run as candidates at their recall election.

Barry Koltnow reported in a "Camera" article dated May 29 that the recall petitions were invalid because they did not contain sufficient grounds to force a recall election. Several hours later the City Council authorized an appeal to the Colorado Supreme Court which agreed to waive oral arguments and expedite its decision. In August, the State Court overturned the District Court decision and declared the recall petitions to be valid and that Mayor Tate and Councilman Fuller could not run as

candidates to replace themselves. In general, the Court held that the State Constitution outweighed the City Charter.

There were other charges and countercharges and participants in the emotional issue, but final resolution came with the recall election held in September.

In January, the City Council named nine women and two men to a special Task Force on Women and Child Care who were given a five-part charge to identify, define and make suggestions to alleviate problems of discrimination and child care. In September, the task force returned with recommendations for the formation of a Women's Resource Center, for an affirmative action program, and a day care program for city employees and others.

The Council funded the Boulder Communications Center for 1974. The Center had been established in 1969 to provide help for transients. Other assistance was provided by the Reverend James McKeown who had set up Haven House near his church. Since the summer of 1970, according to an article in the "Christian Science Monitor" of May 23, "nearly 800 teenagers have found a place on its floor for their sleeping bags and a sympathetic ear for their problems."





# CHAPTER VII

## PUBLIC SAFETY

Through the years, a constant concern of city government has been the safety of its citizens and their property. This includes safety from the elements as well as from people-caused problems.

1965

### FIRE AND POLICE

Boulder's third fire station was dedicated in July at 30th Street and Arapahoe Avenue.

In August, Fire Chief George L. Morse retired after 39 years of service to the city. Dick McCutcheon came out of retirement to fill the vacancy.

#### FLOODS

Boulder city planners, with an eye to recent floods throughout the state, took another look at the city's potential flood danger. The planning and the engineering offices suggested that the 1960 study of problems on Boulder Creek should be utilized and enlarged with studies of the various small tributaries and South Boulder Creek. Among measures suggested for the alleviation of the hazard were the acquisition of flood storage capacity in Barker Reservoir, the development of parkway-levee systems, and the creation of a flood forecasting system for Boulder and South Boulder Creeks.

1966

### FIRE AND POLICE

On the sudden death of Fire Chief McCutcheon, Kenneth Nicholas was named Acting Chief of the Fire Department.

#### FLOODS

In August, Council authorized the retention of Gilbert F. White, a nationally recognized expert on flood damage prevention, to plan for flood plain development controls.

1967

### FIRE AND POLICE

Boulder police estimated in August that there were 5,000 drug users in the city.

#### FLOODS

Zoning and building restrictions for flood plains in Boulder and in the County were among the recommendations made by Gilbert White in January. Dr. White said that rarely can flood hazard be eliminated by protective works, and generally it is difficult to protect all areas in a flood plain by any combination of reservoirs, upstream land improvement and channel improvement. He recommended that the city amend its zoning code to recognize a "floodway" zone which would correspond to areas covered by a 25-year flood. In the flood fringe zone, regulations should require structures to be built to withstand flood flows. He also suggested exploring the possibilities of a cooperative flood-warning system.

#### WINDS

In January, the city suffered losses estimated at a half million dollars in the "worst single windstorm in Boulder history". In March, despite opposition, the City Council adopted an ordinance making it unlawful to fail to use reasonable care to prevent materials or debris from being blown onto another's property by the wind.

1968

#### OFFENSES

In March, the Council made the vagrancy law more precise. A vagrant in Boulder was now a person in a public place under circumstances that gave the impression that he would participate in an imminent violation of the law and could not give credible accounts of his conduct or presence.

The Council, in June, adopted a revised loitering law and several ordinances strengthening enforcement policies in curbing student demonstrations and civil disturbances.

### FIRE AND POLICE

An article dated August 16 by "Camera" reporter Ann Nye said:

A revolutionary plan to partially consolidate the Boulder Police and Fire Departments under a public safety officer program was disclosed today by City Manager Ted Tedesco. The plan ... will save the city more than \$600,000 over the next five years. ... Tedesco said it is designed to solve several critical problems facing the city, including a rising crime rate, a severe shortage of policemen and firemen, high costs of capital construction, a poor response time to fires, demands of firemen for shorter hours, and the rapid growth of the city."

Under the Charter, the City Manager has jurisdiction over administrative departments, one of which is the Department of Public Safety. One factor leading to the consolidation plan was a lack of funds

to provide increases in salary and equipment and personnel for the two departments. The city had reduced the property tax mill levy from 9.90 to 7.70 mills per \$1,000 assessed valuation after the additional one per cent sales tax had been approved in November 1967.

The local chapter of the International Firefighters Association issued a statement that opposed the Public Safety Officer (PSO) program and suggested a referendum so the people could decide whether they would like the change. One major objection by the firemen to the plan was the proposal to reduce the number of men per fire truck from three to two. The City Council agreed in September to form a Council Committee of the Whole to conduct a two-month study of the PSO program after the firemen had objected to a suggestion that a citizen's blue-ribbon committee be appointed to make recommendations to Council. Mayor Robert W. Knecht said:

I firmly believe that the City Council, composed of the elected representatives of the people of our city, is the proper body to consider this matter. In the normal course of events, a proposal such as this, with its budgetary implications, would be considered as a part of the Council's deliberations of the upcoming year's budget.

In October, the Council agreed to proceed with the PSO plan as an experiment for one year. The firefighters began circulating petitions seeking an election on the consolidation measure, on reduction of their work week and on shorter hours and higher pay.

After negotiations with the administration, the firemen agreed to withdraw the second set of their petitions. The parties agreed to bargain in the spring for pay raises in 1970 and agreed to a reduction in hours from sixty-seven-and-a-half to sixty-two, and a plan for further reduction to a fifty-six- hour work-week by 1971.

In mid-December, petitions bearing 5,301 signatures seeking a Charter amendment to prohibit consolidation of the Police and Fire Departments were

filed. The City Attorney's office began drafting an ordinance setting February 11, 1969 for a special election on the Charter change.

### EMERGENCY MEDICAL SERVICE

Boulder now had two ambulance services. Only one, Boulder Ambulance Service, a private company, met the qualifications for a city ambulance license. The other was a volunteer group, the Boulder Volunteer First Aid and Rescue Squad.

1969

### FIRE AND POLICE

In a "Camera" article dated January 5, Pete Goter reported that Chief Donald H. Vendel just finished his first full year as the city's chief law enforcement officer.

With the recent addition of a "training officer", ... Boulder will have its first police academy program. ... The police department takes up two floors of the short end of the T-shaped Municipal Building, the same spot it has had since the building opened in 1952. ... The department gave up keeping prisoners more than a year ago. City arrests are now lodged in county jail ... .

In January, after a week of meetings, city and firefighters' association officials signed an agreement which cancelled the February 11 Charter amendment election. A program of intensive training for Boulder policemen in firefighting was completed in April.

A mass disturbance occurred in the Hill business district in October. An estimated 300 to 400 young people thronged the area. Also in October, at least 3,000 Boulder citizens and University students held a candlelight march from City Hall to Macky Auditorium to protest the Vietnam War.

Funds for two additional narcotics detectives were appropriated in November. Among the reasons given were that during the first six months of

1969, the Police Department's two narcotics detectives seized more hallucinogens and amphetamines than the Denver Police Department. In addition, a new law which was to be in effect in 1970 decreed that the District Court hold preliminary hearings for all persons charged with felonies. These court appearances were expected to nearly double the work load of the detectives.

Fire Chief Kenneth Nicholas, injured in an airplane crash in June, retired December 24. Peter J. Ringleman was appointed to fill the position.

#### FLOODS

In May, central Boulder was hit by a flood estimated at a 25-year intensity. As a consequence, flood plain regulations were moved ahead faster, and were ready in August to provide the guidelines for building and development throughout the County. Earlier studies of Boulder and Bear Creek were scheduled for updating to include more recent development before their floodways could be determined.

The organizational meeting of the new Urban Drainage and Flood Control District of the Denver area was scheduled.

#### WINDS

On January 7 and January 31, there were two windstorms which caused extensive property damage. On both occasions, the wind exceeded 100 miles per hour and damage to residences was estimated to be over \$1 million.

1970

### FIRE AND POLICE

Crimes in Boulder showed an increase of 40 per cent over 1968, and of nearly 60 per cent during the first six months of 1970 over the same period in 1969. Crimes of violence increased more than 70 per cent during the same time.

In March, a Boulder police car was bombed in the municipal parking lot. The University was offering a reward for information leading to the arrest of those involved in the bombing of the ROTC office on campus during the same week.

Heroin traffic in Boulder, virtually non-existent in 1969, had reached startling proportions in 1970. In December, Boulder authorities seized nearly \$200,000 worth of LSD.

With the proliferation of crime came overcrowding of the law enforcement and the corrections systems. In August, Boulder's 80-man police force moved out of the Municipal Building to quarters at 2633 30th Street.

In a report to the Council, the police-fire cooperative plan was said to be a positive addition to the city's public safety program.

#### CORRECTIONS

The Boulder County Undersheriff was quoted as saying that in 1970, more than 3,500 arrests were processed through the county jail and about one-half were drug-related. Most of the drug cases in the courts were for the sale of drugs or the possession of a large quantity. "A liberal court system in Boulder County has increased the drug problem."

A committee seeking a solution to the courts and jail space problems held a public hearing in March. Ron Tollefson of the "Camera" reported that District Court Judge Barnard favored a move to a new location, possibly the county-owned site at Broadway and Iris Avenue. Several lawyers and businessmen urged that the courts be kept downtown since many legal offices are maintained there and property values would suffer if lawyers had to move to be near the justice center.

In May, the committee recommended moving the county administrative offices out of Courthouse Square, using the Courthouse solely for court expansion, and adding a wing to the jail-sheriff's office complex.

#### FLOODS

In September, Jane Cracraft reported in the "Denver Post" that a major study of flood danger in south Boulder had been completed by the Wright-McLaughlin Engineering firm which indicated it would cost \$9.7 million to correct drainage problems on creeks in that part of the city.

#### WINDS

In January, the City Manager's office published the results of an "Analysis of damage to residential properties from high winds occurring in January ... 1969". In February, a gust of wind registered 115 miles per hour on a meter at the National Center for Atmospheric Research.

1971

#### OFFENSES

City Attorney Walt Wagenhals was directed by the Council to prepare a measure setting a series of minimum penalties for street obstruction as well as a range of laws dealing with the "street people" situation. In June,

the Council requested further study of the ordinance and asked the City Attorney to move quickly to draft a law setting penalties for "habitual" offenders. In July, the minimum penalty ordinance was passed. The measure would apply to persons convicted of charges stemming from a third instance of violation of any of 11 city laws within a two-year period.

Terry Aragon, Municipal Court Administrator, said in July that, counting all types of offenses, including traffic, the number of cases in Municipal Court increased from 36,426 in 1970 to 60,545 in 1971.

### FIRE AND POLICE

The Police Department established a branch station on the Hill in April. Apparently as a result of increased patrol activity and arrests, an angry mob took over the area and damaged much of the block between 13th Street and Broadway on the night of Saturday, May 22. On Sunday, mobs gathered in smaller groups and created disorders. About 105 people were jailed.

During 1971, there were 817 narcotics-related arrests by Boulder police.

#### CORRECTIONS

Boulder County's jail was being taxed to its capacity. Sheriff Brad Leach thought the best solution would be the construction of a new jail north of the current site and linked to the courts by an underground tunnel. An alternative plan to join with the city in the construction of a jail raised some opposition from the County Commissioners.

In September, architect Hobart Wagener was considering several design options for a new courts-jail complex on the north side of Courthouse Square. In November, yielding to concerns expressed by Judge Barnard, Sheriff Leach, and several civic groups, the County hired the architectural

consulting firm of Hellmuth, Obata and Kassabaum, Inc. (HOK) to work with the local architect in designing the complex.

### EMERGENCY MEDICAL SERVICE

The Bonded Security Service, Inc., which had been awarded an exclusive 10-year revocable permit in January, became the A-1 Ambulance Service in September. The city offered a maximum subsidy of \$2,600 annually to cover charges or calls which it initiates and which turn out to be uncollectible.

1972

#### OFFENSES

By unanimous vote in September, the Council adopted a new law which outlined when police could jail suspects pending trial and when they should issue court appearance summonses.

### FIRE AND POLICE

As many as 3,000 people roamed the streets of the city and took over the intersection at Baseline Road and U.S. Highway 36 in May. The protest was one of the most militant demonstrations since the strike that closed the University campus in 1970. The activities were in response to President Nixon's announcement of the United States blockade of North Vietnam and the mining of Haiphong Harbor. In a statement released to the press on May 12, the City Council and the City Manager expressed acute awareness of citizen concern over the events during the week. They also expressed unanimous approval and gratitude to the Boulder Police Department for the restraint and sensitivity with which it reacted to the occurrence.

In July, a \$350,000 lawsuit was filed in District Court by four members of a family against the city, the county, and five sheriff's

officers. The charges stemmed from an incident during the anti-war demonstrations on May 9.

From January 1 until December 1, 35 per cent of the city's total burglaries and 55.5 per cent of its assaults with a deadly weapon took place on University Hill. In June, the Council approved 15 additional officers to patrol the Hill at a cost of \$15,000. They also approved a \$9,000 allocation to the Boulder Council of Churches for social work and a self-help program for the area. One of the most noticeably successful programs was the free meal provided daily in Eben G. Fine Park. The total project, known as "Summer-Hill '72", started in December 1971 and was initially backed by Councilmen Tim Fuller and Kenneth Wright. In the fall, the project was termed a success by Police Chief Donald Vendel, and the Council agreed to resume financial support from October through June.

#### CORRECTIONS

Architectural consultants recommended in March that the seven acres of land known as the "ruins" near Sixth Street and Canyon Boulevard be purchased for the new courts-jail complex. The cost of the land was quoted at \$770,000.

Although Boulder voters had refused to increase their sales tax in June, City Manager Ted Tedesco asked the County Commissioners to consider the city's joining in the space and the costs of the new facility sometime in the future when the city could find the necessary funds. Estimated construction costs were now \$3.5 million. The initial projection had been \$2.7 million. By October the cost estimates for the complex were \$4.69 million and the Council had pledged city participation, with its share at \$873,000. In a study session, the Council discussed using part of federal revenue-sharing funds rather than a bond issue to finance the city's share.

The city property tax levy for 1973 was set at 12.8 mills with six-tenths of a mill earmarked for the courts-jail building. The County Commissioners had allocated three mills from the County's Public Works budget over three years for the project.

#### WINDS

On January 11, hurricane-force winds hit Boulder and caused an estimated \$3 million in damage. About 40 mobile homes in north Boulder were destroyed or damaged. In March, approval was given an ordinance requiring tie-down systems for mobile homes. The new regulations required compliance by August 1.

1973

### FIRE AND POLICE

Arson was established as the cause of fires accounting for over 65 per cent of the dollar loss to property in 1973.

The Hill police task force was evidently successful in reducing crime in the area. The sub-station which began operations in March 1971 was closed in September 1973. Complaints and crimes decreased and the situation became less explosive.

A monetary settlement was reached in November in the suit against law enforcement personnel. The family who had brought the lawsuit over injuries sustained during the May 1972 anti-war demonstrations was paid by the insurance companies that insured the Boulder Police Department and the Boulder County Sheriff's Department.

Councilman Kenneth Wright initiated the idea that led to an in-depth management study of the Police Department. The suggestion was made to try

to make the department more cost-effective and able to provide better protection.

In October, the question of whether or not a police officer can be demoted because of private conduct was before the District Court. In December Judge William Neighbors ordered an officer reinstated to his former rank and ruled that a section of the Police Department's regulations was unconstitutional.

#### CORRECTIONS

The County Commissioners were unanimous in their approval of a \$5,888,000 bid in October to construct the Boulder County Justice Center. The total contribution of the city towards that cost was \$1,035,800. Of that amount, \$957,800 was for construction of the building, \$30,000 was for construction of a portion of Sixth Street and the renovation of the Sixth Street bridge for flood control purposes, and \$48,000 was to cover plant investment fees (PIF) for utilities. The city's share came from revenue-sharing funds and the general operating fund.

#### FLOODS

According to an article dated February 14 by "Colorado Daily" reporter Tim Lange, the Council heard a presentation by the Army Corps of Engineers on the methods of flood control along Boulder Creek. A member of the committee on flood control, Dr. Gilbert White, said he was convinced that the Corps' cost figures were too low. He suggested interim steps be taken to control flooding since it might take 12 to 17 years to complete a Corps project once it was approved. Mayor Richard McLean said, "We feel most people in Boulder would place a much higher value on social and environmental aspects than (the Corps) are able to give us."

In April, the City Council gave the administration approval to move

forward with a plan to form a \$500,000-a-year flood control utility. The utility was to be financed by a monthly service charge. An additional surcharge would be levied against homes which would most directly benefit from the program. Second reading of the ordinance, expected in August, did not occur until November.

According to an article by "Camera" reporter Larry Knudsen dated December 4, four Boulder residents filed a lawsuit against the city accusing the administration of issuing building permits in violation of the 1969 flood plain regulations. The District Court suit alleged that the city's Public Facilities Coordinator had failed to delineate flood regulatory areas on official maps as required by the flood plain regulations.

### EMERGENCY MEDICAL SERVICE

The city contributed \$10,000 (\$5,000 in cash and \$5,000 in services) to the A-1 Ambulance Company during this year.

1974

### FIRE AND POLICE

Boulder's crime rate decreased in 1974 by almost nine per cent. This was despite the seizure of \$1.5 million worth of marijuana at the airport in January; despite the bombings which hit the Courthouse and the University security offices in March, the destruction of the Boulder Valley Education Center by arson on February 23, and the bombing two days later of the Flatirons Elementary School. Within three days in May, two car explosions killed six people in two separate incidents. They were said to be premature detonations.

The Police Department hired its first female patrol officer in February.

In March, the Police Benefit Association asked for an investigation into alleged illegal or improper activities conducted by the Police Department staff. Their request was based on allegations that some officers had been assigned to report on the personal life-style of other officers. Chief Vendel viewed it as an effort to discredit his administration. Low morale and a change of attitude within the department reflected a growing civil rights movement among law enforcement agencies.

"Camera" staffer Barry Koltnow reported in an article dated November 22 that, despite the fact that his name had become synonymous with the Police Department's code, Chief Vendel said it was compiled in 1967 by the entire senior staff. Chief Vendel acknowledged that the world had moved to the point where "personal rights have to take preference over departmental rules and regulations", an attitudinal shift which caused the recent furor and which conflicted with his vision of police work. "You must enforce certain rules because it reflects back on the department and the people have an image of police being upright and moral people."

In October, the Police Chief announced he planned to retire after nearly 24 years on the force. He had succeeded Myron M. Teegarden on March 31, 1967. Chief Vendel had been cleared of the alleged misuse of funds from an LEAA grant by the United States Attorney's fraud unit.

The Council passed Boulder's new burglar alarm ordinance in October. It required burglar alarm system owners to register their systems and set a sliding scale of fines for setting off false alarms. Two separate studies had determined that 98 per cent of all alarms in the city were false.

At the same meeting they unanimously authorized a contract for a regional fire training center.

#### FLOODS

Service charges from the city's Flood Control Utility were delayed because the second reading of the ordinance had not come until November 1973. A provision that all specific projects of the long-range program must be brought to the people before action could be taken was added after a bitter fight. Only one-third of the estimated cost was expected to be raised by the fees; the remaining two-thirds was anticipated from state and federal revenues.

The lawsuit brought in late 1973 that would have prevented the city from allowing construction in the flood plain was dismissed in March. A Boulder attorney, Ruth Wright, filed a brief appealing the dismissal.

According to an article by Ken Frizell in the "Camera" dated November 20, the Council informally accepted a study that had been requested by the City Manager. It involved a dispute that arose when Councilman Kenneth Wright charged that the city's Flood Control Utility Director had acted improperly in developing a set of floodway delineation maps. The staff member was cleared, the Councilman's methods were questioned, and the investigator was chastised for interjecting himself into city policy determinations. A motion, based on his "interjection" that Council delay the issuance of building permits in the floodway until after they had studied the flood control regulations, was defeated. The City Council decided to take up other unanswered questions in the investigation at a later date, if at all.

Aside from the flood plain issue, the entire episode raised questions concerning the City Manager's relationship to the City Council and whether or not he had the authority to investigate a member of the Council. In an

article in the "Colorado Daily" on November 15, Timothy Lange said that City Manager Archie Twitchell and Flood Control Director Ted Dieffenderfer asserted that Councilman Kenneth Wright was abusing his legislative role by interfering with administrators. Among additional questions raised by the (Michael) White report were whether several developments (including the University's married student housing project) were improperly given building permits; and whether the city staff misinformed Council about the number of pending permits in the floodway last November.
